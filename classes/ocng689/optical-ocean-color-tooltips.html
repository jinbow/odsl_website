1. Introduction

What “ocean color” means, and why it is a hard inverse problem

1.1 Brief introduction to remote sensing of ocean color

Ocean color remote sensing is the satellite-based measurement of sunlight that has interacted with the atmosphere and the ocean, with the goal of retrieving the water-leaving signal and using it to infer biogeochemical and optical properties of the upper ocean (e.g., chlorophyll-a as a proxy for phytoplankton biomass, particulate and dissolved matter, and water clarity).

A practical framing:

  1. The sensor measures top-of-atmosphere (TOA) radiance/reflectance .
  2. We apply atmospheric correction to estimate the ocean signal.
  3. We convert that signal into remote-sensing reflectance (Rrs) and then into geophysical products.

A central quantity is remote-sensing reflectance, Rrs(λ) (units: sr⁻¹), commonly defined above the surface as:

Rrs(λ) = Lw(λ) / Ed⁺(λ)

where Lw(λ) is water-leaving radiance and Ed⁺(λ) is downwelling irradiance just above the sea surface.

Why it’s difficult: Over most of the open ocean, the ocean is a dark target in the visible; at the satellite, the atmospheric signal is often much larger than the water-leaving signal. This is why atmospheric correction frequently dominates the uncertainty budget.
Interpretation reminder: “Ocean color” is not just “a pretty RGB image.” It is a quantitative retrieval chain: radiometry → atmospheric correction → Rrs → bio-optical inversion → products.

1.2 Foundational concepts and must-to-know points

Radiometry and geometry

  • Radiance (L) vs irradiance (E) , and why satellites measure radiance.
  • Viewing/illumination geometry: solar zenith, sensor zenith, relative azimuth.
  • Reflectance-like quantities used in processing:
    • TOA reflectance (often stored as rhot)
    • Remote-sensing reflectance (Rrs)

Optical properties and the inverse problem

Inherent optical properties (IOPs) include:

  • Absorption: a(λ)
  • Scattering: b(λ)
  • Backscattering: bb(λ)

Apparent optical properties (AOPs) include Rrs(λ) and diffuse attenuation Kd(λ) .

Case-1 vs Case-2 waters (useful heuristic)

  • Case-1 waters
  • Case-2 waters

Atmospheric correction essentials

The sensor sees contributions from:

  • Rayleigh scattering (molecules)
  • Aerosol scattering/absorption
  • Gaseous absorption (e.g., ozone)
  • Surface effects: sun glint , whitecaps
  • Water-leaving signal (the target)

Reprocessing is normal

  • Calibration updates, improved ancillary data, and algorithm advances drive reprocessing.
  • For new missions, early products can be explicitly marked as preliminary; always track processing version and release notes.

2. Review of EM functions relevant to ocean color

Radiometry, attenuation, and scattering regimes

Ocean color is primarily passive visible/near-IR remote sensing: sunlight is modified by absorption and scattering in the atmosphere and ocean.

Photon energy and wavelength

E = hc / λ

Shorter wavelengths (blue/UV) carry higher photon energy and experience stronger Rayleigh scattering (∼λ⁻⁴). Longer wavelengths in the NIR and SWIR are crucial for aerosol characterization and atmospheric correction.

Planck function (thermal emission context)

Ocean color uses reflected solar radiation, but Planck’s law underpins why thermal emission dominates in the IR:

B(λ,T) = (2hc²/λ⁵) · [exp(hc/(λkT)) − 1]⁻¹

Beer–Lambert attenuation

I(z,λ) = I(0,λ) · exp[ −K(λ) · z ]

This motivates why blue-green penetrates deeper than red and why optical depth matters in both atmosphere and water.

Rayleigh vs Mie scattering

  • Rayleigh: molecular scattering, strong wavelength dependence (∼λ⁻⁴).
  • Mie: aerosol scattering with weaker wavelength dependence and strong phase-function effects; often the dominant uncertainty in atmospheric correction.

Fresnel reflection and sun glint

Specular reflection at the air–sea interface depends on incidence angle and surface roughness; wind-driven wave slope statistics are used to model glint.

3. Data flow and processing pipeline

From counts to chlorophyll

3.1 Product levels (typical NASA/OBPG workflow)

Product “levels” provide a standardized way to describe processing maturity: L0–L4 . 

Instrument counts (L0)
  → reconstruction + time-ordering (L1A)
  → calibration + geolocation (L1B)
  → (optional) resampling / gridding (L1C)
  → atmospheric correction + bio-optical retrievals (L2)
  → spatial binning + temporal compositing (L3)
  → model assimilation / derived fields (L4)

3.2 The TOA radiance budget (conceptual)

A useful decomposition of TOA radiance Lt(λ) is:

Lt = Lr + La + Lra + t·Lw + Lg + Lwc
  • Lr: Rayleigh path radiance
  • La: aerosol path radiance
  • Lra: Rayleigh–aerosol multiple scattering term
  • t·Lw: transmitted water-leaving radiance
  • Lg: sun glint
  • Lwc: whitecap/foam contribution

3.3 Typical L1B → L2 processing steps

  1. Radiometric calibration: counts → radiance; apply instrument corrections (dark current, nonlinearity, stray light, polarization sensitivity).
  2. Geolocation: compute lat/lon and pixel geometry.
  3. Ancillary ingestion: ozone, pressure, winds (glint/whitecaps), aerosol/meteorological fields.
  4. Atmospheric correction: remove Rayleigh, correct absorbing gases, estimate aerosols, correct surface effects, mask clouds/ice.
  5. Derive Rrs(λ): water-leaving radiance normalized by downwelling irradiance.
  6. Apply in-water algorithms: chlorophyll, IOPs, Kd, POC, CDOM proxies, etc.
  7. QC + uncertainty: quality flags, uncertainties, diagnostics.
Operational mental model: L1B is what the sensor “saw.” L2 is what we infer about the Earth system from those measurements.

4. Major retrieval algorithms

Atmospheric correction + bio-optical inversion

Ocean-color retrieval is best viewed as two coupled inverse problems: (1) atmospheric correction and (2) bio-optical inversion of the water signal.

4.1 Atmospheric correction (heritage open-ocean approach)

(A) Rayleigh correction

Rayleigh scattering is well-modeled with geometry and pressure; this step is typically high accuracy.

(B) Aerosol correction (often dominant uncertainty)

Classical open-ocean processing often uses a near-IR “black pixel” assumption to estimate aerosol reflectance in NIR, then extrapolates to VIS using aerosol models and multiple-scattering corrections.

(C) Coastal/turbid extensions

In turbid waters, NIR is not black. Strategies include SWIR-based corrections (where water absorption is strong) and leveraging hyperspectral and polarimetric constraints.

4.2 Empirical chlorophyll algorithms

OCx band-ratio family

A widely used approach relates chlorophyll-a to a blue-to-green reflectance ratio via a log-polynomial model:

log10(Chl) = a0 + a1·R + a2·R² + a3·R³ + a4·R⁴

where R is the log of a selected maximum band ratio (blue/green).

Color Index (CI) for oligotrophic waters

For very low chlorophyll, band ratios can be noisy; CI uses a three-band reflectance difference that can be more stable.

Naming collision: PACE’s sensor is called OCI . The chlorophyll algorithm “OCI/CI” in the literature refers to an Ocean Color Index concept.

4.3 Semi-analytical IOP inversion (Rrs → IOPs → constituents)

  • GSM family
  • QAA
  • GIOP

4.4 Common Level-2 products derived after Rrs

  • AOPs: Kd(λ), PAR-related fields, water clarity indices
  • BGC proxies: POC/PIC, bloom indices, CDOM-related products
  • IOPs: a(λ), bb(λ), etc.

5. Major pitfalls and sources of uncertainty

How products fail, and how to diagnose

5.1 Atmospheric and surface contamination

  1. Aerosol model mismatch
    • Symptom: biased Rrs spectrum, often strongest in the blue.
    • Consequence: chlorophyll bias and spurious gradients.
  2. Absorbing aerosols and thin cirrus
    • Symptom: depressed blue; patchy artifacts; negative Rrs in short wavelengths.
  3. Sun glint
    • Symptom: bright streaks; elevated reflectance across bands; chlorophyll artifacts.
  4. Whitecaps/foam
    • Symptom: positive reflectance bias under high winds.
  5. Adjacency effects (coasts)
    • Symptom: nearshore contamination from land-reflected photons scattered into the sensor view.

5.2 In-water algorithm limitations

  • Case-2 waters: CDOM/sediments can mimic pigment absorption effects; global chlorophyll algorithms can fail.
  • Shallow water bottom reflectance: can produce unrealistically high green/red reflectance and nonsensical inversions.
  • Vertical structure: satellites see an optically weighted near-surface layer, not a depth-resolved concentration profile.
  • Physiology/community variability: chlorophyll is a proxy; pigment packaging and C:Chl can vary strongly.

5.3 Instrument and processing issues

  • Calibration drift / vicarious calibration differences: sub-percent changes can matter for ocean products.
  • Stray light and spectral cross-talk: particularly important for hyperspectral instruments.
  • Polarization sensitivity: can alias into radiometry if not corrected.
  • Reprocessing/version changes: always report processing version; early mission products may be preliminary.

5.4 Validation/matchup pitfalls

  • Space–time mismatch between in situ sampling and satellite overpass.
  • Representativeness errors: point measurements vs 1 km-scale pixels.
  • Differences in in situ protocols/instruments for radiometry and pigments.

6. Major ocean-color missions in history

Continuity, cross-calibration, and climate records

Ocean color has a multi-decade record. Building consistent climate records requires careful merging of sensors with different spectral bands, calibration strategies, and viewing geometries.

Selected ocean-color missions and why they matter
Mission / Sensor Agency Years (approx.) Key contributions / notes
CZCS (Nimbus-7) NASA (US) 1978–1986 Proof-of-concept global ocean color; demonstrated large-scale biological signals.
OCTS NASDA/JAXA (Japan) 1996–1997 Bridged the gap between CZCS and the continuous modern record.
SeaWiFS NASA/OrbView-2 (US) 1997–2010 Established a long, stable time series; foundational algorithms and validation.
MODIS (Terra/Aqua) NASA (US) 1999– / 2002– Extended continuity; broad synergy with SST and atmosphere products.
MERIS (Envisat) ESA (Europe) 2002–2012 Coastal-capable band set; strong role in merged products.
VIIRS (S-NPP, NOAA-20, …) NOAA/NASA (US) 2011–present Operational continuity and multi-sensor climate-quality streams.
OLCI (Sentinel-3A/3B) ESA/EUMETSAT 2016–present Operational series; coastal emphasis; strong continuity strategy.
GOCI (geostationary, regional) KARI (Korea) 2010–present High-frequency diurnal sampling over regional seas; enables process studies.

7. Introduction to PACE

Hyperspectral ocean color + polarimetry

PACE is designed to advance ocean-color biogeochemistry and atmospheric science jointly, improving our ability to correct atmospheric effects and interpret ocean ecosystem change.

7.1 Instruments

  • OCI — a hyperspectral UV–VIS–NIR instrument with SWIR bands for atmospheric correction/aerosol work.
  • SPEXone — multi-angle polarimetry to improve aerosol/cloud characterization.
  • HARP2 — wide-angle imaging polarimetry for aerosols/clouds and surface properties.

7.2 What changes relative to heritage missions?

  • Hyperspectral OCI enables improved discrimination of phytoplankton spectral features and more flexible inversion constraints.
  • Polarimetry provides stronger aerosol constraints, potentially reducing one of the largest uncertainty sources in ocean color.
  • UV–NIR–SWIR coverage expands correction and retrieval options in challenging waters (coastal, turbid, absorbing aerosols).
Data mindset: Always track processing version and flags. For any mission, early releases can change materially as calibration and algorithms mature.

8. Practical examples with PACE

Data discovery, download, and plotting L1B → L2

8.1 Data access

Most PACE ocean-color products are distributed via OB.DAAC / OceanColor/OBPG systems. A common workflow is:

  1. Search granules using OB.DAAC File Search (GUI or API).
  2. Authenticate with Earthdata Login .
  3. Download Level-1B and Level-2 (OC_AOP and OC_BGC) for the same granule.

Example: programmatic search (illustrative)

BASE="https://oceandata.sci.gsfc.nasa.gov/api/file_search"

curl -G "$BASE"   --data-urlencode "sensor_id=PACE_OCI"   --data-urlencode "dtype=L2"   --data-urlencode "search=OC_BGC"   --data-urlencode "sdate=2024-04-15"   --data-urlencode "edate=2024-04-16"   --data-urlencode "results_as_file=0"
Important: Parameter strings (sensor_id, dtype, suite names) can evolve; confirm against current OB.DAAC File Search documentation.

8.2 Plotting Level-1B (TOA) vs Level-2 (Rrs and chlorophyll)

Goal: visually demonstrate what atmospheric correction accomplished by comparing a TOA reflectance slice ( Level-1B ) with an Rrs slice ( Level-2 ), and then mapping a derived geophysical product (chlor_a).

Python environment setup

conda create -n pace-oc python=3.11 -y
conda activate pace-oc
pip install numpy xarray netCDF4 matplotlib cartopy dask

Helper: open netCDF groups safely

PACE/OBPG files are netCDF4 and often use groups such as geophysical_data and navigation_data. 

import xarray as xr

def open_group(fn, group):
    """Open a netCDF group (OBPG-style netCDF4 with groups)."""
    return xr.open_dataset(fn, group=group, decode_cf=True, mask_and_scale=True)

Plot Level-1B TOA reflectance near 443 nm

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

l1b_file = "PACE_OCI.20240415T150000.L1B.nc"  # replace with your file

geo   = open_group(l1b_file, "geolocation_data")
obs   = open_group(l1b_file, "observation_data")
bands = open_group(l1b_file, "sensor_band_parameters")

lat = geo["latitude"]
lon = geo["longitude"]

wl_blue = bands["blue_wavelength"].values
i443 = int(np.argmin(np.abs(wl_blue - 443)))

# rhot_* are TOA reflectances
rhot_443 = obs["rhot_blue"].isel(blue_bands=i443)

plt.figure()
plt.pcolormesh(lon, lat, rhot_443, shading="auto")
plt.title("PACE OCI L1B: TOA reflectance (rhot) near 443 nm")
plt.xlabel("Longitude")
plt.ylabel("Latitude")
plt.colorbar(label="unitless (TOA reflectance)")
plt.show()
Interpretation: Bright features in L1B can be clouds, haze, glint, or aerosols—not chlorophyll. You are still looking at TOA.

Plot Level-2 Rrs near 443 nm and chlorophyll

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

l2_aop = "PACE_OCI.20240415T150000.L2.OC_AOP.nc"  # replace
l2_bgc = "PACE_OCI.20240415T150000.L2.OC_BGC.nc"  # replace

# AOP: Rrs spectrum + wavelengths
aop_band = open_group(l2_aop, "sensor_band_parameters")
aop_geo  = open_group(l2_aop, "navigation_data")
aop_data = open_group(l2_aop, "geophysical_data")

wl3d = aop_band["wavelength_3d"].values
i443 = int(np.argmin(np.abs(wl3d - 443)))

lat = aop_geo["latitude"]
lon = aop_geo["longitude"]

Rrs_443 = aop_data["Rrs"].isel(wavelength_3d=i443)

plt.figure()
plt.pcolormesh(lon, lat, Rrs_443, shading="auto")
plt.title("PACE OCI L2 OC_AOP: Rrs near 443 nm")
plt.xlabel("Longitude")
plt.ylabel("Latitude")
plt.colorbar(label="sr$^{-1}$")
plt.show()

# BGC: chlorophyll-a (mg m^-3)
bgc_geo  = open_group(l2_bgc, "navigation_data")
bgc_data = open_group(l2_bgc, "geophysical_data")

chl = bgc_data["chlor_a"]

plt.figure()
plt.pcolormesh(lon, lat, np.log10(chl), shading="auto")
plt.title("PACE OCI L2 OC_BGC: log10(chlor_a)")
plt.xlabel("Longitude")
plt.ylabel("Latitude")
plt.colorbar(label="log10(mg m$^{-3}$)")
plt.show()

Plot an Rrs spectrum at one pixel (hyperspectral advantage)

row, col = 800, 600  # choose a pixel of interest

rrs_spec = aop_data["Rrs"].isel(number_of_lines=row, pixels_per_line=col).values

plt.figure()
plt.plot(wl3d, rrs_spec)
plt.title("Rrs spectrum at one pixel")
plt.xlabel("Wavelength (nm)")
plt.ylabel("Rrs (sr$^{-1}$)")
plt.show()
What to look for: In open ocean, Rrs is typically higher in blue and decreases toward red. In coastal/turbid water, green/red can increase depending on particles and CDOM.

8.3 Going from L1B → L2 with SeaDAS/OCSSW

To reproduce or modify standard processing, use SeaDAS and OCSSW tools (e.g., l2gen ). 

# Illustrative example only — exact options depend on your SeaDAS/OCSSW install and PACE configuration.

l2gen   ifile=PACE_OCI.20240415T150000.L1B.nc   ofile=PACE_OCI.20240415T150000.L2.OC_AOP.nc   l2prod="Rrs_nnn aot_865 angstrom"   glint_opt=1

8.4 Mini-lab checklist

  1. Find one PACE OCI scene over a region you care about (e.g., Gulf of Mexico, coastal Texas).
  2. Download one L1B and matching L2 OC_AOP and L2 OC_BGC for the same granule.
  3. Plot
    • L1B rhot_blue near 443 nm
    • L2 Rrs near 443 nm
    • L2 chlor_a (log scale)
  4. Explain (3–5 sentences):
    • What looks atmospheric in L1B that largely disappears in L2?
    • Where do you still see algorithm struggle (glint, clouds, coast)?
