Key concepts you should connect to orbit shape

Orbit shape is not just geometry—it is how we encode the satellite’s energy and distance-from-Earth variability. The cards below summarize the minimum set of ideas you should be able to state, sketch, and compute.

Conic sections shape

Two-body orbits are conic sections defined by e: circle (e=0), ellipse (0<e<1), parabola (e=1), hyperbola (e>1). Earth satellites are almost always ellipses.

See terms

Semi-major axis a scale

a sets the orbit’s size and (for bound orbits) the period via Kepler’s third law. Larger a implies longer period and generally higher altitude.

Try adjusting a

Eccentricity e shape

e controls how “stretched” the ellipse is. For fixed a, increasing e lowers perigee and raises apogee, increasing altitude variation across an orbit.

Try adjusting e

Perigee & apogee geometry

rp=a(1−e) and ra=a(1+e). These are distances from Earth’s center. Altitudes are h=r−RE.

Explore rp/ra

Speed varies along the orbit dynamics

By the vis-viva equation, the satellite moves fastest at perigee and slowest at apogee. Shape therefore influences where time is spent, which can matter for dwell time and sampling.

Watch vp vs va

True anomaly ν & ω where you are

ν tells you where you are on the orbit relative to perigee; ω rotates where perigee sits in the orbital plane. Neither changes the ellipse’s size.

Move ν
Remote-sensing connection: for Earth observation, altitude strongly influences footprint size and swath, while orbit period and repeat characteristics influence revisit time and sampling patterns.
