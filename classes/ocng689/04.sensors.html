Introduction to Sensors for Satellite Oceanography

This module introduces the major satellite sensor families used in oceanography, organized by the electromagnetic (EM) spectrum and grouped into passive and active sensing.

The guiding question throughout is: what does the sensor actually observe, and what ocean physics makes that observation informative? You will repeatedly see the same physical ingredients—radiative transfer, dielectric properties, surface roughness, wave spectra, specular reflection, and atmospheric propagation—reappear across different instruments.

EM framework: what satellites actually measure

From photons/radio waves to geophysical variables

1) The remote sensing measurement chain

Satellite oceanography is fundamentally an inference problem. Instruments measure an electromagnetic signal at the sensor. We then infer an ocean variable by modeling how the EM field was generated and modified on its path: 

Source → (Atmosphere) → Ocean interaction → (Atmosphere) → Sensor → Retrieval model → Geophysical product

Passive:   Sun or thermal emission             Lλ or TB measured at the sensor
Active:    Transmitted pulse/waveform          σ⁰ / phase / delay / waveform measured at the sensor

Passive sensing (radiometry)

Measures naturally occurring radiance.

  • Solar-reflective bands (UV–VIS–NIR–SWIR): mostly reflected sunlight → ocean color
  • Thermal IR: emitted radiance from the ocean “skin” → sea surface temperature (SST)
  • Passive microwave: brightness temperature (TB) → emissivity/temperature/roughness effects (e.g., salinity, wind)

Active sensing (radar/lidar)

Transmits energy and measures the return.

  • Lidar/laser (optical): time-of-flight photons → elevation, waves, (sometimes) bathymetry
  • Radar (microwave): backscatter (σ⁰), phase, delay/Doppler → winds, roughness features, sea level, waves, sea ice

2) Why wavelength matters: the ocean–EM interaction “menu”

Wavelength controls which physical mechanism dominates (and therefore what the measurement is sensitive to). For ocean remote sensing, it is useful to distinguish three interaction regimes:

Volume optics (UV–SWIR)

Light penetrates and scatters within the upper water column. The signal depends on absorption and scattering by water, phytoplankton, dissolved organic matter, and particles.

Key idea “Water-leaving radiance” is a small residual after atmospheric correction.

Skin emission (thermal IR)

The ocean emits thermal radiation; IR senses the top tens of micrometers (“skin”) and requires careful correction for atmospheric absorption and cloud contamination.

Key idea IR SST is usually high resolution but clear-sky only.

Boundary roughness & dielectric (microwave)

Microwave signals come from the near-surface boundary and depend strongly on the seawater dielectric constant and the sea-surface roughness spectrum (from capillary to gravity waves, foam).

Key idea Many radar products are “wind/roughness first,” then derived variables.

Unifying ocean-surface concept: The sea surface is a spectrum of waves (capillary and gravity). Many microwave sensors are sensitive to specific roughness scales. In particular, off-nadir radars often respond strongly to Bragg-resonant short waves, while nadir altimeters emphasize quasi-specular facets that satisfy the specular reflection geometry.

3) Ocean wave physics you will reuse (minimal toolkit)

The sea surface contains waves across a wide range of wavelengths. Two facts are especially useful for interpreting microwave measurements:

  • Capillary–gravity waves: for short waves, surface tension matters. The combined dispersion relation is often written (conceptually) as ω² = gk + (σ/ρ)k³, where k is wavenumber, g gravity, σ surface tension, and ρ density. Winds generate these short waves quickly, and they respond rapidly to changes in wind stress.
  • Bragg (resonant) waves: for a radar of wavelength λradar viewing at incidence angle θ, first-order Bragg resonance emphasizes surface waves of wavelength approximately λB ≈ λradar / (2 sin θ). These are typically centimeter-scale for Ku/C-band radars and are strongly tied to local wind forcing and damping by surfactants.

You do not need to derive these relations yet—but you should practice the habit of asking: “What roughness scale (millimeters? centimeters? meters?) does this instrument care about?”

4) Practical “observables vocabulary” (what you should name first)

  • Radiance (Lλ) and reflectance → ocean color
  • Brightness temperature (TB) → passive microwave (emissivity + temperature)
  • Backscatter coefficient (σ⁰) → radar roughness/structure
  • Range/delay (time-of-flight) → altimetry/lidar elevations
  • Waveform shape → wave height, tracking, surface type
  • Phase/Doppler → motion sensitivity (coherent radars; SAR specifics)

Capillary–gravity waves and Bragg resonance

Why centimeter-scale roughness ties radar backscatter to local winds (and slicks)

In active microwave remote sensing (scatterometers and SAR), much of the measured backscatter is controlled by very short surface waves—typically centimeter-scale. Two linked ideas are central: (1) the physics of capillary–gravity waves (restored by gravity and surface tension), and (2) Bragg resonance, which describes how a radar preferentially “selects” a narrow band of surface wavelengths that efficiently backscatter energy to the antenna.

Capillary–gravity waves: gravity + surface tension as restoring forces

For surface waves, the restoring force that flattens perturbations depends on wavelength. Long waves are primarily restored by gravity, while very short waves are increasingly restored by surface tension (because curvature becomes large). In deep water, the combined dispersion relation is commonly written:

ω2 = gk + (σ/ρ)k3,    k = 2π/λ.

Dispersion (deep water) g: gravity,  σ: surface tension,  ρ: density,  k: wavenumber

What the two terms imply

Gravity-dominated (longer waves): ω2 ≈ gk
Tension-dominated (shorter waves): ω2 ≈ (σ/ρ)k3

As k increases (wavelength decreases), the k3 term grows rapidly, so surface tension becomes dynamically important. This is why centimeter-scale roughness can behave differently than swell.

Crossover scale to remember

Set gk ≈ (σ/ρ)k3 ⇒ kc ≈ √(ρg/σ), and therefore

λc ≈ 2π/kc = 2π√(σ/(ρg)) ≈ 1.7 cm (order of magnitude).

Around λ ∼ 1–2 cm, gravity and surface tension are comparably important—right in the range emphasized by many microwave radars. These short waves have sub-second periods, so their energy levels can adjust rapidly when wind stress changes.

Remote-sensing implication: Winds can generate and maintain centimeter-scale waves quickly, so the short-wave “tail” is often closely tied to the local wind field. Conversely, surfactants (natural films, oil slicks) can strongly damp these short waves, reducing microwave backscatter and producing “dark” signatures in SAR.

Bragg (resonant) waves: which surface wavelengths a radar emphasizes

A coherent radar does not respond equally to all ocean waves. In many geometries, the dominant first-order backscatter is well explained by Bragg resonance—constructive interference from surface ripples that act like a weak diffraction grating. For a monostatic radar with wavelength λradar viewing at incidence angle θ (from nadir), the resonant surface wavelength is often approximated by:

kB = 2kr sinθ,   kr = 2π/λradar,   ⇒   λB ≈ λradar / (2 sinθ).

First-order Bragg λB: resonant surface wavelength “selected” by the radar geometry

The key conceptual point is that “Bragg waves” are not a special physical wave type—they are simply capillary–gravity waves at the resonant scale (i.e., λ ≈ λB). Because λB is typically in the centimeter range for common ocean radars, Bragg scattering provides a direct pathway from wind-driven short-wave roughness to measured backscatter σ0.

Radar band (typical) λradar λB at θ = 20° λB at θ = 35° λB at θ = 45°
Ku-band (scatterometry common) ∼ 2.2 cm ∼ 3.2 cm ∼ 1.9 cm ∼ 1.6 cm
C-band (SAR + scatterometry common) ∼ 5.6 cm ∼ 8.2 cm ∼ 4.9 cm ∼ 4.0 cm
Practical takeaway: If the local wind strengthens, Bragg-scale roughness generally increases and backscatter tends to rise (with directionality tied to wind direction). If surfactants damp the Bragg-scale waves, backscatter drops—one reason slicks are often conspicuous in SAR imagery and can bias wind retrievals.
Concept checkpoint: how “small” and “large” waves both matter

A useful mental model is the “two-scale” picture: longer waves tilt the local surface (changing the effective incidence angle and facet orientation), while the radar’s coherent return is strongly controlled by the centimeter-scale Bragg waves riding on those facets. In practice, both the short-wave spectrum and larger-scale modulation (gusts, fronts, currents, slicks) can shape the observed radar backscatter patterns.

Passive sensors (high frequency → low frequency)

Solar-reflective → thermal emission → microwave emission

Passive ocean remote sensing is radiometry: the instrument measures EM energy that already exists (sunlight reflected by the ocean/atmosphere, or thermal emission from the Earth system). The key is that passive sensors do not control illumination, so the signal strength and contamination pathways differ by band. 

High frequency / short λ
  UV–VIS–NIR–SWIR (solar reflective)  → ocean color / water constituents
  Thermal IR (emitted)                → sea surface temperature (skin)
  Passive microwave (emitted)         → salinity, wind effects, rain/ice (all-weather emphasis)
Low frequency / long λ

A) UV–VIS–NIR–SWIR radiometry (Ocean Color)

What it observes: spectral radiance dominated by atmosphere; the ocean signal is a small residual.

Observable

Top-of-atmosphere (TOA) spectral radiance Lλ in narrow bands. The target geophysical quantity is often expressed as remote-sensing reflectance, Rrs(λ) = Lw(λ) / Ed(λ), where Lw is water-leaving radiance and Ed downwelling irradiance.

Ocean physics link

The water-leaving signal depends on the balance of absorption and scattering in the upper water column. Inherent optical properties (IOPs) include absorption a(λ) and backscattering bb(λ), contributed by water molecules, phytoplankton pigments, suspended particles, and CDOM.

What you infer

Chlorophyll-a proxies, particle backscatter/TSM, CDOM indicators, water clarity, bloom dynamics, optical water types, and (with care) some coastal habitat indicators.

How the signal is formed (conceptual)

  • Atmosphere first: At TOA, most measured radiance is from atmospheric scattering (Rayleigh + aerosols) and sunglint. Ocean color retrieval begins by removing the atmospheric contribution (atmospheric correction).
  • Ocean contribution: Photons that enter the ocean are absorbed/scattered. A small fraction is scattered back out of the surface and reaches the sensor. The spectral shape of Rrs(λ) contains information about water constituents.
  • Penetration depth: Optical sensing is a volume measurement: blue-green bands can sample deeper in clear water; red/NIR are quickly attenuated. Your “effective depth” is on the order of one optical attenuation length (varies strongly by water type).
Where ocean physics shows up most clearly: Optical sensors “see” changes in a(λ) and bb(λ). That is why pigment absorption features, particle scattering slopes, and CDOM absorption in the blue are central to retrieval strategies.

What can go wrong (overview)

  • Clouds/aerosols: mischaracterized aerosols bias atmospheric correction.
  • Sun glint: specular reflection from facets (wind-dependent) adds radiance unrelated to water constituents.
  • Adjacency effects: land/case-2 water reflectance can contaminate coastal pixels.
  • Vertical structure: subsurface maxima can decouple surface color from depth-integrated biomass.

Deeper dive: full radiative transfer framing, atmospheric correction algorithms, bio-optical models, and validation belong in the dedicated Ocean Color module/week.

B) Thermal Infrared radiometry (Sea Surface Temperature)

What it observes: emitted thermal radiance from the ocean surface “skin” (clear-sky emphasis).

Observable

Thermal IR radiance that is often converted to a brightness temperature via the Planck function (conceptually, “the temperature a blackbody would need to match the observed radiance in that band”).

Ocean physics link

IR emission originates from the skin layer (top tens of micrometers). The skin can differ from bulk temperature because of the cool-skin effect and diurnal warm-layer formation.

What you infer

High-resolution SST patterns, fronts/eddies, upwelling signatures, coastal thermal gradients, and temperature context for air–sea flux estimation (with additional meteorological inputs).

How the signal is formed (conceptual)

  • Emission + emissivity: The ocean emits strongly in thermal IR and has high emissivity (near—but not exactly—1). Small emissivity errors can become meaningful SST biases.
  • Atmospheric absorption: Water vapor (and other gases) absorb IR. Many SST algorithms use “split-window” bands to correct for water vapor effects.
  • Clouds dominate: Even thin clouds can block or contaminate the IR signal. Cloud detection and masking are not optional; they are central to the product.
Interpretation habit: When you read “SST” from IR sensors, ask: is this skin SST or a “foundation”/bulk estimate? How is diurnal warming handled? What cloud screening was applied?

What can go wrong (overview)

  • Cloud contamination: residual cloud leads to cold biases (typical).
  • Atmospheric correction errors: water vapor variability affects retrieval.
  • Skin–bulk mismatch: physical mismatch matters for flux and in situ comparisons.

Deeper dive: Planck-based retrieval details, split-window physics, cloud detection, and skin/bulk comparisons belong in the dedicated IR/SST module/week.

C) Passive microwave radiometry (Salinity, winds, precipitation/ice context)

What it observes: brightness temperature governed by emissivity (dielectric + roughness) and physical temperature, with all-weather capability.

Observable

Multi-frequency, multi-polarization brightness temperatures (TB). In simple terms, TB reflects how efficiently the surface emits (emissivity) multiplied by physical temperature, plus atmospheric contributions.

Ocean physics link

Microwave emissivity depends strongly on the seawater dielectric constant, which varies with salinity and temperature. Surface roughness and foam modify emissivity by changing the distribution of facets and adding emitting/scattering elements.

What you infer

Sea surface salinity (especially L-band), wind-speed-related roughness corrections, rain/ice signatures, and large-scale hydrologic/cryospheric context for ocean forcing and freshwater fluxes.

How the signal is formed (conceptual)

  • Dielectric control: At long microwave wavelengths (notably L-band), small changes in salinity alter the dielectric constant enough to measurably change emissivity and therefore TB.
  • Roughness/foam correction: Wind-generated roughness increases effective emissivity. Whitecaps/foam emit strongly and must be accounted for, typically using auxiliary wind information or multi-frequency constraints.
  • Atmosphere is different (not absent): Compared with optical/IR, clouds matter far less, but water vapor and especially rain can contribute at higher microwave frequencies. “All-weather” does not mean “no correction needed.”
Connection to Bragg-wave physics: Passive microwave radiometers are not “Bragg scatterers” in the same direct way as radars, but they still respond to the same wind-driven short-wave roughness field (and foam) that also controls radar backscatter. In practice, many microwave retrievals succeed because they model how the sea-surface wave spectrum reshapes emissivity across frequency and polarization.

What can go wrong (overview)

  • Radio-frequency interference (RFI): can corrupt L-band measurements.
  • Roughness/foam biases: mis-modeled wind/sea state impacts salinity retrievals.
  • Land/ice contamination: large footprints near coasts/ice edges challenge interpretation.

Deeper dive: emissivity modeling, dielectric physics, polarization, RFI mitigation, and practical SSS/wind retrievals belong in the dedicated Passive Microwave module/week.

Active sensors (high frequency → low frequency)

Lidar/laser → microwave radar bands (Ka → Ku → X → C → L)

Active sensors transmit energy and measure the return. In oceanography, active sensing is dominated by lidar/laser (optical) and radar (microwave). The key advantage is that the instrument controls illumination; the tradeoff is that interpretation must account for transmit/receive geometry, incidence angle, polarization, and the physics of scattering. 

High frequency / short λ
  Optical active (laser/lidar)         → time-of-flight photons: elevation, waves, sometimes bathymetry
  Ka/Ku-band radar (millimeter–cm)     → altimetry + scattering sensitive to short waves and precipitation
  X/C/L-band radar (cm–dm)             → SAR imaging, scatterometry, roughness/structure, sea ice
Low frequency / long λ

A) Optical active sensing: lidar & laser altimetry

What it observes: time-of-flight and photon returns; strong sensitivity to surface geometry and water optical attenuation.

Observable

Return time (range) and return intensity/counts from laser pulses. In photon-counting systems, individual photons are time-tagged; in waveform systems, the full return pulse shape is measured.

Ocean physics link

Over the ocean, lidar returns are shaped by surface specular reflection, surface slope/roughness, and (for green wavelengths) water-column attenuation and backscatter. Penetration depends on water clarity (often summarized by diffuse attenuation coefficient Kd).

What you infer

Surface elevation tracks, wave statistics in some conditions, sea-ice freeboard, and—where water is clear enough—coastal bathymetry from separated surface and bottom returns (with refraction corrections).

How the signal is formed (conceptual)

  • Time-of-flight geometry: Range is inferred from the travel time of the pulse. Precise orbit and pointing knowledge are essential.
  • Water penetration (for bathymetry): Green light can penetrate into water; the bottom return appears later in time. Depth estimates require correcting for refraction and the reduced speed of light in water.
  • Sea state matters: Rough seas broaden and decorrelate the surface return; glint-like effects occur because the ocean surface is a moving field of facets.
Interpretation habit: For lidar bathymetry, always ask: what sets the penetration limit here—water clarity (Kd), bottom albedo, sun angle/background photons, or sea state?

Deeper dive: photon statistics, geolocation/orbit effects, water-column optics, and bathymetric inversion belong in the dedicated Gravity & Lidar module/week.

B) Microwave radar primer: how the ocean surface creates backscatter

Before sensor types: understand the two dominant surface-scattering pictures—Bragg resonance vs quasi-specular facets.

Bragg-resonant scattering (off-nadir viewing)

For many ocean-viewing radars at moderate incidence angles (typically tens of degrees), backscatter is strongly influenced by short surface waves that satisfy the Bragg condition: λB ≈ λradar / (2 sin θ).

  • These “Bragg waves” are usually centimeter-scale for Ku/C-band sensors.
  • They are generated by local wind stress and respond quickly (minutes) to changes in wind.
  • They are damped by surfactants/oil, which is why slicks often appear dark in radar images.

Quasi-specular facets (near-nadir viewing)

For near-nadir radars (altimetry), the return is dominated by facets oriented to satisfy specular reflection back to the antenna. Sea state affects the distribution of facet slopes and therefore the waveform shape and bias corrections.

  • Large-scale waves modulate facet slopes.
  • Small-scale roughness influences reflectivity and “sea state bias.”
  • Wave height broadens the leading edge of the altimeter waveform.
One sentence summary: Scatterometers and SAR often sense the wind-driven short-wave field (Bragg physics); altimeters sense geometry (range) using quasi-specular returns that depend on sea state.

C) Ka-band radar (highest-frequency microwave): fine-scale surface interaction and altimetry options

Ka-band (millimeter wavelength) is sensitive to small-scale roughness and atmospheric liquid water; used notably in some altimetry concepts.

Observable

Backscatter (σ⁰), range, and waveform metrics depending on instrument design.

Ocean physics link

Shorter radar wavelength shifts Bragg sensitivity toward smaller waves and increases sensitivity to rain/cloud liquid water. Sea state still shapes returns via facet geometry and short-wave roughness.

Typical use cases

High-resolution altimetry approaches (including interferometric concepts), detailed sea state sensitivity in low-rain conditions, and specialized applications where Ka-band’s footprint/processing advantages matter.

Deeper dive: Ka-band propagation and rain impacts, Ka-band altimetry processing, and wide-swath concepts are treated in the Altimetry module/week.

D) Ku-band radar (centimeter wavelength): scatterometry and conventional altimetry

Ku-band is a workhorse frequency: strong ocean backscatter, strong Bragg sensitivity, and broad heritage in altimetry and wind sensing.

Ku-band scatterometry (vector winds)

Observable: normalized radar cross section σ⁰ measured at multiple azimuth angles (and sometimes multiple incidence angles).

How the ocean imprints: Wind stress generates short capillary–gravity waves. The Bragg-resonant components dominate the return, and because those short waves align preferentially with the wind direction, σ⁰ varies with azimuth. Inversion uses geophysical model functions (GMFs) that map σ⁰ to wind speed/direction for given geometry and polarization.

  • Retrieval target: near-surface (often “10 m neutral”) wind vectors over the ocean.
  • Key physics phrase: “Bragg scattering from wind-aligned short waves.”
  • Common confounders: rain (at Ku), coastal land contamination, sea ice.
Bragg waves in practice: If the wind drops suddenly, the short Bragg-scale wave field decays quickly—scatterometer winds often reflect this rapid adjustment. Longer gravity waves decay more slowly; they modulate, but do not solely determine, the scatterometer signal.

Ku-band radar altimetry (sea surface height & waves)

Observable: two-way travel time (range) and echo waveform shape at (near) nadir.

How the ocean imprints: The waveform is shaped by the distribution of surface facet heights/slopes within the footprint. Significant wave height broadens the leading edge; surface roughness affects return power; and subtle electromagnetic effects create sea state bias corrections.

  • Retrieval targets: sea surface height (SSH), significant wave height (SWH), and wind-related metrics from backscatter.
  • Key physics phrase: “Quasi-specular nadir reflection modulated by sea state.”
  • Common confounders: wet/dry troposphere delay, ionosphere, tides, coastal waveform distortion.
Interpretation habit: SSH is never “just range.” It is range plus orbit, plus geophysical corrections, plus a reference surface. The altimeter is precise, but the full product is a correction-and-model ecosystem.

Deeper dive: scatterometer GMFs and ambiguity removal belong in the Active Radar: Scatterometry module/week; waveform models, corrections, geoid/tides, and SSH dynamics belong in the Radar Altimetry module/week.

E) X-band radar: SAR imaging of ocean roughness patterns

What it observes: high-resolution spatial structure in radar backscatter driven by roughness at resonant scales, modulated by ocean dynamics.

Observable

SAR measures complex-valued backscatter (amplitude and phase) to form a focused image. Most ocean interpretation uses calibrated intensity (related to σ⁰) plus contextual geometry (incidence angle, polarization).

Ocean physics link

SAR intensity is strongly tied to Bragg-scale roughness, but the patterns you see often come from modulation of the short-wave field by larger processes: currents, convergence/divergence, internal waves, fronts, rain cells, and surfactant films.

What you infer

Wind streaks, fronts, internal waves, eddies’ surface signatures, slicks/oil spills (via damping), sea ice morphology, ship wakes; and in some cases wave spectra and surface motion proxies.

How SAR “sees” the ocean (conceptual)

  • Bragg-scale sensitivity: SAR backscatter often responds to centimeter-scale waves satisfying the Bragg condition for its frequency and incidence angle.
  • Modulation pathways: Larger ocean processes modify the short-wave field through:
    • Tilt modulation: long waves change local incidence angle (facet orientation).
    • Hydrodynamic modulation: current gradients/convergence alter short-wave amplitude (changing σ⁰).
    • Damping: surfactants reduce short-wave energy → reduced σ⁰ (“dark” slicks).
  • Important caution: Many ocean features are visible because they change roughness—not because SAR directly measures temperature, salinity, or current. The interpretation often requires auxiliary data or dynamics reasoning.
Bragg waves as the “carrier signal” for SAR: Think of the short-wave field as the carrier. Ocean dynamics imprint information by modulating that carrier. This is why SAR is extraordinarily informative, but also why it can be ambiguous without context.

Deeper dive: coherent imaging, polarization, wave-current interactions, Doppler-based products, and quantitative wind/wave retrieval methods belong in the SAR module/week.

F) C-band radar: robust all-weather ocean backscatter (SAR and scatterometry)

C-band is widely used because it balances sensitivity to roughness with manageable atmospheric impacts and strong heritage.

C-band scatterometry

Observable: multi-azimuth σ⁰. Physics: still Bragg-dominated, but with resonant scales shifted relative to Ku-band. Wind direction and speed are inferred via GMFs.

  • Often preferred for reduced rain contamination relative to Ku-band.
  • Wind retrieval logic is the same: azimuthal anisotropy of the Bragg-wave field encodes direction.

C-band SAR

Observable: SAR backscatter images (amplitude/phase). Physics: Bragg-scale roughness sensitivity + modulation by dynamics.

  • Strong utility for sea ice classification and ocean slick detection.
  • Wind/wave retrieval possible with appropriate models and ancillary wind direction constraints.

Deeper dive: C-band is treated alongside other SAR bands in the SAR module/week and alongside wind retrieval fundamentals in the Scatterometry module/week.

G) L-band radar & bistatic reflectometry (low microwave): longer-wave roughness sensitivity and “signals of opportunity”

At longer microwave wavelengths, sensitivity shifts toward longer roughness scales; L-band also enables bistatic approaches (GNSS-R).

Observable

Depending on system: backscatter (σ⁰) for L-band SAR, or delay–Doppler power distributions for GNSS reflectometry.

Ocean physics link

Longer radar wavelengths emphasize longer Bragg-resonant surface waves and can respond differently to vegetation/ice. For GNSS-R, the key concept is the glistening zone around specular reflection whose extent depends on surface roughness.

What you infer

Roughness/wind proxies, sea ice and coastal applications, and in GNSS-R: surface roughness and wind-related metrics from reflected navigation signals (often as complementary/experimental products).

Connecting the dots: L-band is central in passive microwave salinity because dielectric sensitivity is useful there; in active sensing, L-band shifts the resonant roughness scale and enables bistatic geometries. Across both, the common thread is dielectric + roughness physics.

Deeper dive: If included in the course, L-band SAR/reflectometry is best handled as an advanced extension after you master scatterometry and SAR fundamentals.

Spectrum summary: from high → low frequency (passive + active)

A single “mental map” to carry into every later module

1) The spectrum ladder (high ν → low ν)

  1. UV–VIS–NIR–SWIR (Passive) — measures reflected sunlight; retrieves ocean color via absorption/scattering of constituents.
  2. Optical lidar/laser (Active) — measures photon time-of-flight; retrieves elevation/waves; sometimes bathymetry (clear water).
  3. Thermal IR (Passive) — measures emitted radiance; retrieves skin SST (clear-sky constraint).
  4. Ka/Ku-band radar (Active) — measures range/waveforms (altimetry) and/or backscatter; sensitive to short-wave roughness and atmospheric liquid water.
  5. X/C-band radar (Active) — SAR/scatterometry: images and quantifies roughness patterns driven by Bragg-resonant waves, modulated by ocean dynamics.
  6. L-band microwave (Passive + Active) — passive radiometry for salinity; active SAR/reflectometry for roughness and bistatic products.
One-sentence integration: Optical/IR sensors mainly sense water-column optics and skin temperature; microwave sensors mainly sense surface dielectric + roughness (often via Bragg-scale waves) and/or surface geometry (altimetry).

3) A “decision rule” you can use immediately

If you need biogeochemistry…

Start with ocean color. Your primary job becomes atmospheric correction and understanding optical water types.

If you need temperature patterns…

Start with thermal IR SST (high resolution) and consider microwave SST or blended products when clouds dominate.

If you need dynamics (winds/SSH/roughness)…

Start with scatterometry (winds), altimetry (SSH), and SAR (roughness features). Expect Bragg-wave physics and geophysical corrections to be central.

Recommended learning workflow: For each sensor family, practice the sequence: (1) name the observable → (2) state the dominant ocean physics → (3) list the main corrections → (4) identify the product and its resolution/coverage limits.
