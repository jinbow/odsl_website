OCNG 489/689 Midterm Study Guide

Topics covered: EM waves & radiative transfer, sensor types + data levels, optical (visible/NIR) ocean remote sensing, SST retrieval from thermal infrared and passive microwave, plus active radar scatterometry (ocean surface vector winds) and passive microwave applications for sea-surface salinity and winds.

Important topics:

  • Write the forward model — what contributes to the TOA measurement
  • State assumptions/approximations (e.g., emissivity, atmosphere, rain flags)
  • Identify dominant error sources
  • Map a product to its processing level (L1/L2/L3/L4)
  • Convert \(\lambda \leftrightarrow \nu\); interpret Planck/Wien; identify atmospheric "window" regions
  • Distinguish and define irradiance, radiance, reflectance and emissivity
  • Distinguish TOA vs surface contributions
  • Classify active vs passive sensors; swath vs gridded products; Level 0→4 pipeline
  • Describe conceptual steps: IR split-window SST, MW SST regression, scatterometer wind vector inversion
  • Diagnose failure modes: clouds, water vapor, rain, RFI, high winds, coastal land contamination
  • Explain different SST definitions

Quick formula sheet

Memorize meanings + units
  • \[ c = \lambda \nu \]
    (speed of light \(c = 3.00\times10^{8}\) m/s, \(\lambda\) = wavelength, \(\nu\) = frequency)
  • \[ E_{\mathrm{photon}} = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \]
  • Planck (wavelength form) :
    \[ B_{\lambda}(T) = \frac{2hc^{2}}{\lambda^{5}}\,\frac{1}{\exp\!\left(\frac{hc}{\lambda k T}\right)-1} \]
  • Wien:
    \[ \lambda_{\max} \approx \frac{2897\;\mu\mathrm{m\cdot K}}{T} \]
  • Rayleigh–Jeans approximation (\(h\nu \ll k_B T\), microwave limit):
    \[ B_{\nu}(T) \approx \frac{2\nu^{2}k_{B}T}{c^{2}} \;\propto\; T \]
    (radiance linear in \(T\) → \(T_B \approx T_\mathrm{physical}\) in the microwave)
  • Brightness temperature:
    \[ T_{B} = B_{\lambda}^{-1}(L) \]
    (inverse Planck evaluated at the observed radiance \(L\))
  • Radiative transfer (MW-friendly):
    \[ T_{B} \approx \tau\left[ \varepsilon T_{s} + (1-\varepsilon)\,T_{\downarrow}\right] + T_{\uparrow} \]
  • Scatterometer observable:
    \[ \sigma^{0} = \sigma^{0}(\theta,\phi,p) \]
  • Bragg condition (concept):
    \[ \lambda_{\mathrm{surface}} \approx \frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{radar}}}{2\sin\theta} \]
  • Salinity radiometry idea:
    \[ T_{B} \approx \varepsilon(\mathrm{SSS},\mathrm{SST},\theta,\mathrm{pol})\,T_{s} \]
    (plus atmosphere/roughness terms)

Unit discipline: keep track of m vs μm vs cm, and GHz vs THz; many exam errors are unit errors.

EM waves & remote-sensing fundamentals

Windows, absorption, and what the satellite actually “sees”

Key ideas

  • Sensors measure radiance (or backscatter), not “temperature” directly. Retrievals map measurements → geophysical variables via an algorithm + assumptions.
  • Atmospheric windows enable surface sensing: visible/NIR (reflected sunlight), thermal IR ~ 3.7–12 μm (emission near Earth’s thermal peak), microwave cm (emission/backscatter; penetrates most clouds).
  • Polarization matters in microwave: ocean emissivity/backscatter depends on H/V (or VV/HH) and incidence angle.

Classic exam questions

  • Why do IR SST products have gaps in cloudy regions?
  • Why do microwave products have coarser spatial resolution (λ/D diffraction)?
  • How do water vapor and cloud liquid water show up in thermal IR vs microwave?

Sensor types & data levels

Know the taxonomy and the processing pipeline

Sensor types: what distinguishes them?

  • Passive sensors “listen” to natural radiation (solar-reflected in VIS/NIR; thermal emission in IR/MW): radiometers, spectrometers.
  • Active sensors “illuminate” the Earth and measure the return: scatterometer, SAR, altimeter, lidar.
  • Imaging strategy (how you map the Earth): whiskbroom, pushbroom, conical scan.
  • Geometry matters: nadir-looking (IR radiometers), side-looking (SAR/scatterometer), multi-incidence (scatterometer beams).

Data processing levels (NASA convention)

Level What it is Ocean examples you should recognize
L0 Raw instrument packets / counts at full resolution (minimally processed) Telemetry packets; raw radar echoes; uncalibrated detector counts
L1 Calibrated, geolocated instrument measurements (radiances, brightness temps, backscatter) IR radiances; MW \( T_{B} \); scatterometer \( \sigma^{0} \) swaths
L2 Derived geophysical variables at native measurement geometry (usually swath) Swath SST; swath SSS; wind vectors at wind vector cells (WVCs)
L3 Mapped/gridded products (space/time bins, typically daily/monthly) Daily 0.25° gridded winds; 7-day gridded salinity; daily gridded SST
L4 Analysis products (multi-sensor blended and/or model-assimilated fields) GHRSST L4 SST analyses; reanalysis winds; gap-filled SSS maps

Bridge to GHRSST: GHRSST uses “L2P” for sensor-level SST products (skin for IR, subskin for MW), and “L4” for gridded analyses/blends. On an exam, you can usually map GHRSST L2P → NASA L2, GHRSST L3 → NASA L3, GHRSST L4 → NASA L4.

Optical remote sensing (visible/NIR)

Water-leaving radiance, reflectance, and atmospheric correction

What’s measured vs what you want

  • Sensor measures TOA radiance; ocean-color wants water-leaving radiance \( L_{w}(\lambda) \) or remote sensing reflectance \( R_{rs}(\lambda) \).
  • Atmospheric correction removes Rayleigh scattering + aerosols + surface effects (whitecaps, sun glint) to isolate \( L_{w} \).

Key definitions

  • \[ R_{rs}(\lambda) = \frac{L_{w}(\lambda)}{E_{d}^{+}(\lambda)} \]
  • IOPs vs AOPs
  • Common pitfalls: coastal adjacency effects, bottom influence (shallow), glint masking.

Infrared SST (thermal IR)

Skin SST + split/triple-window correction

What IR SST represents

  • IR radiometers (≈3.7–12 μm) measure skin SST (top O(10 μm) of the ocean). This matters for air–sea fluxes and buoy comparisons.
  • Main limitation: cloud opacity → data gaps and potential residual cloud-edge contamination.

Split-window concept (11 μm and 12 μm)

  • Atmospheric absorption differs between ~11 and ~12 μm (mainly water vapor). Using \( (T_{11}-T_{12}) \) corrects water vapor effects.
  • Generic structure:
    \[ \mathrm{SST} = a_1 + a_2\,T_{11} + a_3\,(T_{11}-T_{12}) + a_4\,\sec(\theta)\,(T_{11}-T_{12}) \]
    (coefficients by regression).

Triple-window (nighttime) reminder

  • Often uses 3.7 μm + 11 μm + 12 μm at night to improve atmospheric correction (3.7 μm contaminated by reflected solar during day).

Microwave SST (passive MW)

Subskin SST + wind/roughness corrections

What MW SST represents

  • Microwave radiometers (often ~6–11 GHz for SST sensitivity) estimate subskin SST (upper mm scale), with much better sampling in cloudy regions (except rain).
  • MW SST is coarser spatially; careful coastal masks are common.

What influences MW brightness temperatures over ocean?

  • Surface emission: depends on SST and emissivity.
  • Roughness & foam: wind speed changes emissivity and polarization mixing.
  • Atmospheric terms: water vapor and cloud liquid water emit/absorb; rain can dominate and breaks retrievals.

Exam-friendly narrative: MW retrieval is a multi-channel separation problem: disentangle SST, wind/roughness, and atmospheric contributions using frequency/polarization diversity.

Scatterometry key points

Active radar for ocean surface vector winds

What a scatterometer measures

  • Scatterometers are active microwave radars: they transmit pulses and measure the returned power from the sea surface.
  • The core observable is the NRCS σ0, which depends on incidence angle, azimuth (relative to wind), and polarization.
  • Physical basis: wind → surface roughness (capillary + short gravity waves). Under Bragg scattering, σ0 is strongly influenced by resonant short waves whose wavelength depends on radar wavelength and incidence angle.

Why multiple looks are needed (wind direction)

  • σ0 is anisotropic with azimuth relative to wind, so measuring σ0 from multiple azimuth angles allows retrieval of wind direction (and speed).
  • Inversion uses a GMF to find wind vectors consistent with the set of σ0 observations.
  • Because the inversion is not unique, retrieval produces ambiguities that must be removed.

Typical products and levels

  • L1: calibrated σ0 swaths (sometimes per-beam).
  • L2: wind vectors on a wind-vector-cell grid (e.g., 25 km or 12.5 km).
  • L3: gridded daily/monthly wind fields.

Representative missions (know at least 2)

  • QuikSCAT / SeaWinds (Ku-band): very wide swath; excellent coverage of ice-free oceans.
  • ASCAT on MetOp satellites (C-band): operational winds; generally less rain-sensitive than Ku-band.
  • Historical/other: NSCAT, RapidScat (good to recognize by name).

Failure modes / error budget themes

  • Rain contamination (especially Ku-band): changes backscatter and attenuates the radar signal → biased winds; handled by rain flags but not perfect.
  • Low winds (< ~3 m/s): weak anisotropy and low SNR.
  • High winds: potential saturation and foam effects; direction can degrade.
  • Coasts/sea ice: land/ice contamination and non-ocean scattering.
Scatterometer wind retrieval (conceptual workflow)
L0 packets → L1 σ0 calibration/geolocation → GMF inversion → multiple wind ambiguities
→ ambiguity removal (spatial consistency + NWP background; e.g., 2DVAR) → L2 winds → L3 grids

Passive microwave applications beyond SST

Sea-surface salinity & winds

Sea-surface salinity (SSS) from space: why L-band?

  • Satellite SSS is retrieved primarily using L-band radiometry near 1.413 GHz (~21 cm), where ocean emissivity is sensitive to salinity via the seawater dielectric constant.
  • Sensitivity is small (Tb changes are subtle), so the retrieval is calibration- and correction-heavy.

Key corrections/complications

  • SST dependence: dielectric constant depends on temperature and salinity, so SST is an essential ancillary.
  • Surface roughness/wind: modifies emissivity; some missions use additional channels or an ancillary sensor to correct roughness.
  • RFI detection/mitigation is critical.
  • Ionospheric effects (e.g., Faraday rotation) can affect polarization, particularly at L-band.

SSS missions to recognize

  • SMOS (ESA): L-band interferometric radiometer (MIRAS).
  • Aquarius (NASA/CONAE): L-band radiometers; included a scatterometer channel to help correct roughness.
  • SMAP (NASA): L-band radiometer (salinity via dedicated algorithms; RFI mitigation is a big theme).

Passive microwave winds

  • Most passive MW radiometers retrieve wind speed (not full vector) over ocean from wind-driven changes in emissivity due to roughness/foam.
  • Multi-frequency + polarization diversity helps separate wind speed from atmospheric water vapor/cloud liquid water effects.
  • WindSat is a key example of a polarimetric radiometer designed to retrieve wind vectors (speed + direction) from passive MW.

Trade-offs you can explain

  • Higher frequencies can have stronger wind sensitivity but are more affected by rain.
  • Lower frequencies penetrate rain better but have lower intrinsic sensitivity and coarser footprints.
  • Near coasts: land contamination is a major limitation for all microwave products.

Key comparisons you should be able to write

One-paragraph answers that earn points

SST: thermal IR vs passive MW

  • IR SST: high spatial resolution, best accuracy in clear-sky; measures skin SST; cloud-limited.
  • MW SST: better sampling under clouds (except rain), coarser footprint; measures subskin SST; sensitive to wind/roughness.

Winds: scatterometer vs passive MW radiometer

  • Scatterometer (active): wind vector (speed + direction), based on σ0 anisotropy; rain can bias (especially Ku-band); generally higher spatial detail than passive MW winds.
  • Passive MW: wind speed (and sometimes direction with polarimetry), based on emissivity changes due to roughness/foam; also rain-sensitive; often used for all-weather wind speed fields.
  • In situ (buoys): point measurements; comparisons require careful collocation and height/neutral-stability conventions (10 m winds).

Practice questions

A. Quick calculations / sanity checks

  1. Compute ν for λ = 11 μm (thermal IR window). State units clearly.
  2. Using Wien’s law, estimate λmax for T = 290–300 K. Why does this matter for IR SST band choice?
  3. Given a microwave frequency of 6.9 GHz, compute λ. Explain (qualitatively) why the footprint is large.

B. Concepts: forward model thinking

  1. Write the one-layer MW brightness temperature expression and label surface emission, reflected downwelling atmosphere, and upwelling atmosphere.
  2. Explain why split-window SST uses (T11 − T12). What does a larger difference often imply?
  3. For ocean color: why is TOA radiance over ocean dominated by the atmosphere? What is “atmospheric correction” removing?

C. Sensor types + data levels

  1. Classify each as active or passive: scatterometer, IR radiometer, microwave radiometer, SAR, lidar, altimeter.
  2. Decide the processing level (L0–L4): (i) calibrated σ0 swaths, (ii) swath SST, (iii) daily gridded 0.25° winds, (iv) multi-sensor blended SST analysis.
  3. What extra processing step separates L1 → L2 for scatterometry? (Hint: inversion using a GMF + ambiguity removal.)

D. Scatterometry

  1. Define σ0 and list the three geometry/polarization variables it depends on (θ, φ, p). Why do we need multiple azimuth views?
  2. Explain what a GMF is. Why does the inversion produce multiple wind-direction ambiguities?
  3. Name two major error sources in scatterometer winds and describe how products usually flag them.

E. Passive microwave salinity & winds

  1. Why is L-band (~1.413 GHz) used for sea-surface salinity from space? What makes the retrieval hard?
  2. List at least three corrections needed for SSS retrieval (e.g., SST dependence, wind/roughness, RFI, ionosphere).
  3. Explain the physical reason wind speed can be retrieved from passive microwave brightness temperatures over ocean.
