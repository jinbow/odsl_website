Electromagnetic Waves in Satellite Oceanography

Satellite oceanography is, at its core, a measurement-physics discipline: satellites measure electromagnetic (EM) radiation and we infer ocean variables by modeling how that radiation is generated, modified, and detected. This chapter establishes the EM-wave toolkit that you will use repeatedly for ocean color, sea-surface temperature, sea-surface salinity, winds, waves, and sea-surface height.

1. Why EM Waves Matter for Satellite Oceanography

Satellites measure radiation; we infer ocean variables through an end-to-end physical chain

1.1 Remote Sensing as an Inference Chain

A useful mental model for satellite oceanography is an inference chain. The ocean variable you care about (e.g., chlorophyll, SST, salinity, winds) is not observed directly. Instead, it modifies an EM signal that travels through the atmosphere and into a sensor, where it is converted into numbers.

Course theme: You will repeatedly identify which part of the chain dominates error and how "corrections" work.

2. EM Wave Fundamentals

A minimal physics toolkit: definitions, relationships, and propagation

2.1 Wave Descriptions and Terminology

EM radiation can be described as a traveling wave with electric and magnetic field components. In most ocean remote-sensing contexts, we can treat EM waves as propagating at a speed close to the speed of light in vacuum.

Key Wave Quantities:

Quantity Symbol Definition Units
Wavelength λ Distance between repeating wave crests meters (m)
Frequency ν Cycles per second Hz
Period T Time per cycle (T = 1/ν) seconds (s)
Angular frequency ω ω = 2πν rad/s
Wavenumber k k = 2π/λ rad/m

3. The EM Spectrum and Ocean-Relevant Windows

Band choice is driven by ocean signal physics and atmospheric transmission

3.1 Spectrum Regions Used in Ocean Remote Sensing

Satellite oceanography uses a subset of the EM spectrum. The most common regions are:

UV/Visible (ocean color)VIS

Sunlight reflected from the ocean; sensitive to pigments and particles.

NIR/SWIRNear/Shortwave IR

Atmospheric correction, land/cloud screening, glint handling; also useful in turbid waters.

Thermal IR (TIR)SST

Ocean skin temperature under clear skies.

MicrowavePassive

Passive emission (SSS, winds, sea ice); operates day/night and through many clouds.

RadarActive

Active backscatter (scatterometry, SAR) and range/time delay (altimetry).

5. EM Interaction with the Ocean

How the air–sea interface and seawater determine what a sensor can observe

5.1 Air–Sea Interface Physics

The air–sea interface is where many remote-sensing signals are created and modified. Incoming radiation can be reflected and transmitted; outgoing radiation can be emitted or scattered.

5.2 Visible/NIR: Penetration and Water-Leaving Radiance

In the visible (and some NIR) region, sunlight can penetrate the ocean and interact with constituents in the water column. The sensor receives a mixture of water-leaving radiance, surface reflection, and atmospheric path radiance.

5.3 Thermal IR: The "Skin" Temperature Concept

Thermal infrared radiation from the ocean originates from an extremely thin near-surface layer (the "skin") because water absorbs strongly in the IR. IR sensors measure a skin temperature representative of the upper tens of micrometers of the ocean surface.

5.4 Microwave Emission and Radar Backscatter

At microwave wavelengths the ocean is an efficient emitter but also a highly conductive medium, so penetration depths are typically on the order of millimeters. The observed signal is therefore dominated by the sea surface and its dielectric/roughness properties.

7. Atmospheric Effects

The atmosphere modifies and adds radiation between ocean and sensor

7.1 Radiative Transfer Concept

Signal at sensor ≈ (surface contribution) × (atmospheric transmission) + (path radiance) + (atmospheric emission)

Key insight: The atmosphere is not just "noise." It is an active component of the measurement equation.

7.2 Scattering Processes

Rayleigh Molecular Scattering

Strong wavelength dependence (~λ⁻⁴), very important in the blue. Major contributor to ocean color atmospheric path radiance.

Aerosol Mie Scattering

Weaker wavelength dependence; highly variable. Often the dominant uncertainty in ocean color atmospheric correction.

Clouds Contamination

Dominant contamination in VIS and TIR. Cloud masking is a first-order step in many products.

7.3 Key Absorbing Gases

Water Vapor (H₂O)IR/MW

Strong absorption features across IR and microwave; key for IR SST correction and microwave attenuation/emission.

Ozone (O₃)UV/VIS

Important in UV and parts of visible spectrum.

Oxygen (O₂)MW

Strong microwave absorption bands useful for atmospheric profiling.

Worked Examples

Calculation toolkit aligned to HW 1
Example 1: Wavelength of a 6.8 GHz Channel Microwave

Problem: A microwave radiometer has a channel at 6.8 GHz. What is its wavelength?

Solution: λ = c/ν

• Convert: 6.8 GHz = 6.8 × 10⁹ Hz

• Use c ≈ 3.00 × 10⁸ m/s

• Compute: λ ≈ (3.00 × 10⁸) / (6.8 × 10⁹) ≈ 4.41 × 10⁻² m = 4.41 cm

Interpretation: A wavelength of a few centimeters is in the microwave regime; it is sensitive to surface emission and roughness.

Example 2: Photon Energy at 550 nm vs 11 μm VIS vs TIR

Problem: Compare photon energies for green light (550 nm) and thermal IR (11 μm).

Solution: E = hc/λ

• 550 nm = 5.50×10⁻⁷ m → E ≈ 3.61×10⁻¹⁹ J ≈ 2.25 eV

• 11 μm = 1.10×10⁻⁵ m → E ≈ 1.81×10⁻²⁰ J ≈ 0.113 eV

Interpretation: Visible photons carry much higher energy than thermal IR photons. Visible remote sensing is usually reflected solar; thermal IR is emitted radiation.

Example 3: Viewing Angle and Path Length Geometry

Problem: If viewing zenith angle increases from 0° (nadir) to 60°, how does the atmospheric path length change?

Solution: m ≈ 1/cos(θ)

• At θ = 0°: m = 1/cos(0) = 1

• At θ = 60°: m = 1/cos(60°) = 1/0.5 = 2

Interpretation: At 60° zenith angle, path length is doubled, increasing atmospheric correction challenges.

Graduate Extension: Atmospheric Correction & Error Budgets

Move from "identify effects" to "evaluate impacts on retrieval accuracy"

10.1 What an Atmospheric Correction Is

An "atmospheric correction" is not just subtracting something off the measurement. In general, it is an inversion problem in which we attempt to separate coupled contributions:

• Surface term(s) (what we want)

• Atmospheric term(s) (scattering/absorption/emission)

• Instrument/geometry effects

Graduate-level mindset: Many corrections require assuming an aerosol model, a water vapor profile, or a cloud screening rule. Those assumptions can generate bias.

10.2 Error Sources Framework

Random Noise

Tends to average down with repeated sampling.

Bias Systematic Error

Does not average down; can generate spurious trends and spatial artifacts.

Representativeness Error

Mismatch between what satellite samples (skin, footprint, time) and what in situ samples.

Uncertainty Propagation

For a retrieved variable y = f(x₁, x₂, …), a first-order uncertainty propagation is:

σ²y ≈ Σ (∂f/∂xi)² σ²xi    (+ covariance terms when needed)

Key Takeaways

Core concepts to remember

Measurement Chain

Satellites measure radiation; ocean variables are inferred through models (surface + atmosphere + instrument + retrieval).

Band Choice = Physics Choice

Different bands probe different processes and depths (water column vs skin vs surface dielectric/roughness).

Polarization Adds Information

Helps separate competing effects, especially in microwave/radar measurements.

Atmosphere is Active

Scattering/absorption/emission must be modeled and corrected—it's part of the measurement.

Core Equations

c=λν and E=hc/λ are used constantly for band interpretation and calculations.

Graduate Focus

Atmospheric correction assumptions often dominate accuracy; evaluate error budgets and bias explicitly.
