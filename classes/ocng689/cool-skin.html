Sea-surface temperature (SST) definitions by effective sampling depth

Sea-surface temperature is not uniquely defined because the upper ocean exhibits strong near-surface vertical structure:

  • a molecular/laminar “skin” layer in the upper hundreds of micrometers to millimeters, and
  • a diurnally varying warm layer that can extend through the upper few meters under weak winds and strong insolation. For satellite–in situ consistency, SST is therefore specified by the effective sampling depth (or the physical layer it represents).
    • Minnett et al. (2019)

    Common SST definitions by depth/layer

    Interface temperature (SSTint) air–sea interface

    Temperature exactly at the air–sea interface on molecular scales (a conceptual boundary value that cannot be sampled directly by typical in situ thermometers). (Minnett et al., 2019)

    Skin SST (SSTskin) ~10–20 μm

    The temperature sensed by infrared radiometers (typical wavelengths 3.7–12 μm), representing the conductive, diffusion-dominated sublayer at ~10–20 μm depth. Because of the cool-skin effect (net upward heat flux under most conditions), SSTskin is commonly cooler than the water just beneath; an average skin temperature drop of ~0.17 K has been reported (larger at very low winds). (Minnett et al., 2019)

    Subskin SST (SSTsubskin) base of laminar sublayer

    Temperature at the base of the conductive laminar sublayer (below most of the cool-skin gradient). In practice it can be approximated by passive microwave radiometers operating around ~6–11 GHz, whose electromagnetic sampling depth is deeper than the infrared skin layer and therefore less dominated by the cool-skin signal. (Minnett et al., 2019)

    SST at depth (SSTz or SSTdepth; “bulk SST”) cm–m

    Water temperature measured below SSTsubskin at a specified depth z (centimeters to meters), e.g., by drifting buoys, moored sensors, or ship intakes. The measured value depends on sensor depth (ship intakes often ~2–10 m, whereas some near-surface thermistors are at a few centimeters). When the upper ocean is well mixed, SSTdepth is close to mixed-layer temperature; during diurnal stratification it may differ substantially from SSTskin/SSTsubskin. (Minnett et al., 2019)

    Foundation SST (SSTfnd) diurnally “unperturbed” reference

    A diurnally “unperturbed” reference SST, defined as free of diurnal temperature variability. In a GHRSST-based convention, SSTfnd is defined as the temperature at the first time of day when absorbed solar heating exceeds surface heat loss—a practical proxy for the baseline temperature from which the diurnal warm layer develops. (Minnett et al., 2019)

    How these definitions relate

    Cool-skin + warm-layer decomposition

    A standard view is that the temperature relevant to air–sea exchange (and infrared radiometry) can be expressed as a bulk/depth temperature plus a diurnal warm-layer term and a cool-skin term; this decomposition provides a pathway to convert subsurface (“bulk”) temperatures to interfacial/skin temperature estimates. (Fairall et al., 1996)

    Magnitude matters: Diurnal warming at the surface can be large. Shipborne radiometric observations report maximum warming of 5.2 K, and in the early morning there is often little diurnal warming so subskin and bulk temperatures converge—consistent with the idea of a foundation-like baseline. (Gentemann & Minnett, 2008)
