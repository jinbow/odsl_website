Introduction

What “ocean color” means, and why it is a hard inverse problem

1.1 Brief introduction to remote sensing of ocean color

Ocean color remote sensing is the satellite-based measurement of sunlight that has interacted with the atmosphere and the ocean, with the goal of retrieving the water-leaving signal and using it to infer biogeochemical and optical properties of the upper ocean (e.g., chlorophyll-a as a proxy for phytoplankton biomass, particulate and dissolved matter, and water clarity). The features identified in ocean color can be used not only to study biological processes but also the bio-physical coupling (e.g., Liu et al., 2016).

A practical framing (recurring theme):

  1. The sensor measures top-of-atmosphere (TOA) radiance/reflectance .
  2. We apply atmospheric correction to estimate the ocean signal.
  3. We convert that signal into remote-sensing reflectance (Rrs) and then into geophysical products.

A central quantity is remote-sensing reflectance, \(R_{rs}(\lambda)\) (units: sr\(^{-1}\)), commonly defined above the surface as:

\[ R_{rs}(\lambda) = \frac{L_w(\lambda)}{E_d^+(\lambda)} \]

where \(L_w(\lambda)\) is water-leaving radiance and \(E_d^+(\lambda)\) is downwelling irradiance just above the sea surface.

Why it’s difficult: Over most of the open ocean, the ocean is a dark target in the visible; at the satellite, the atmospheric signal is often much larger than the water-leaving signal. This is why atmospheric correction frequently dominates the uncertainty budget.
Interpretation reminder: “Ocean color” is not just “a pretty RGB image.” It is a quantitative retrieval chain: radiometry → atmospheric correction → \(R_{rs}\) → bio-optical inversion → products.

Review of EM functions relevant to ocean color

Radiometry, attenuation, and scattering regimes

Ocean color is primarily passive visible/near-IR remote sensing: sunlight is modified by absorption and scattering in the atmosphere and ocean. The following is a brief review of key EM concepts. For more details, see the EM theory module.

Photon energy and wavelength

\[ E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \]

Shorter wavelengths (blue/UV) carry higher photon energy and experience stronger Rayleigh scattering (\(\sim \lambda^{-4}\)). Longer wavelengths in the NIR and SWIR are crucial for aerosol characterization and atmospheric correction.

Planck function (thermal emission context)

Ocean color uses reflected solar radiation, but Planck’s law underpins why thermal emission dominates in the IR:

\[ B(\lambda, T) = \frac{2hc^{2}}{\lambda^{5}} \cdot \left[\exp\left(\frac{hc}{\lambda k T}\right) - 1\right]^{-1} \]

Beer–Lambert attenuation

\[ I(z,\lambda) = I(0,\lambda)\cdot \exp\left[-K(\lambda)\, z\right] \]

where \(K(\lambda)\) is the attenuation coefficient as a function of wavelength (typical values range from 0.01 to 1 m⁻¹). This motivates why blue-green penetrates deeper than red and why optical depth matters in both atmosphere and water.

Rayleigh vs Mie scattering

  • Rayleigh: molecular scattering, strong wavelength dependence (\(\sim \lambda^{-4}\)).
  • Mie: aerosol scattering with weaker wavelength dependence and strong phase-function effects; often the dominant uncertainty in atmospheric correction.

Fresnel reflection and sun glint

Specular reflection at the air–sea interface depends on incidence angle and surface roughness; wind-driven wave slope statistics are used to model glint.

3. Data flow and processing pipeline

From counts to chlorophyll

3.1 Product levels (typical NASA/OBPG workflow)

Product “levels” provide a standardized way to describe processing maturity: L0–L4 . 

Instrument counts (L0)
  → reconstruction + time-ordering (L1A)
  → calibration + geolocation (L1B)
  → (optional) resampling / gridding (L1C)
  → atmospheric correction + bio-optical retrievals (L2)
  → spatial binning + temporal compositing (L3)
  → model assimilation / derived fields (L4)

3.2 The TOA radiance budget (conceptual)

A useful decomposition of TOA radiance \(L_t(\lambda)\) is:

\[ L_t = L_r + L_a + L_{ra} + t\,L_w + L_g + L_{wc} \]
  • \(L_r\): Rayleigh path radiance
  • \(L_a\): aerosol path radiance
  • \(L_{ra}\): Rayleigh–aerosol multiple scattering term
  • \(t \cdot L_w\): transmitted water-leaving radiance
  • \(L_g\): sun glint
  • \(L_{wc}\): whitecap/foam contribution

3.3 Typical L1B → L2 processing steps

  1. Radiometric calibration: counts → radiance; apply instrument corrections (dark current, nonlinearity, stray light, polarization sensitivity).
  2. Geolocation: compute lat/lon and pixel geometry.
  3. Ancillary ingestion: ozone, pressure, winds (glint/whitecaps), aerosol/meteorological fields.
  4. Atmospheric correction: remove Rayleigh, correct absorbing gases, estimate aerosols, correct surface effects, mask clouds/ice.
  5. Derive \(R_{rs}(\lambda)\): water-leaving radiance normalized by downwelling irradiance.
  6. Apply in-water algorithms: chlorophyll, IOPs, \(K_d(\lambda)\), POC, CDOM proxies, etc.
  7. QC + uncertainty: quality flags, uncertainties, diagnostics.
Operational mental model: L1B is what the sensor “saw.” L2 is what we infer about the Earth system from those measurements.

4. Major retrieval algorithms

Atmospheric correction + bio-optical inversion

Ocean-color retrieval is best viewed as two coupled inverse problems: (1) atmospheric correction and (2) bio-optical inversion . At graduate level, treat both as inverse problems with explicit forward models, uncertainty weighting, and identifiability limits.

Graduate framing: write your retrieval as y = F(x) + ε, where y are measurements (e.g., TOA reflectance or Rrs), x are state variables (aerosols + ocean IOPs/constituents), and ε are errors (instrument + modeling + representativeness). Your algorithm is the choice of F, assumptions on ε, and constraints/priors on x.

4.1 Atmospheric correction (heritage open-ocean approach, with graduate details)

4.1.1 Forward model and TOA reflectance budget

Most operational systems work in a reflectance-like space to reduce sensitivity to solar irradiance magnitude and geometry. A common conceptual decomposition of the TOA reflectance ρt(λ) is:

\[ \rho_t(\lambda) \;=\; \rho_r(\lambda)\;+\;\rho_{a}(\lambda)\;+\;\rho_{ra}(\lambda)\;+\; T(\lambda)\,\rho_w(\lambda)\;+\;\rho_g(\lambda)\;+\;\rho_{wc}(\lambda) \]
  • ρr : Rayleigh path reflectance
  • ρa : aerosol path reflectance
  • ρra : Rayleigh–aerosol coupling
  • T : transmittance multiplying the ocean term
  • ρw : water-leaving reflectance
  • ρg : sun glint
  • ρwc : whitecaps/foam
Key consequence: over clear open ocean, T·ρw is often only a few percent of ρt in the visible—so sub‑percent biases in aerosol treatment can dominate errors in Rrs and derived products.

(A) Rayleigh + gas corrections (usually “high confidence” components)

Rayleigh scattering is computed from known physics using viewing geometry and surface pressure. Gaseous absorption (notably ozone in the visible/UV, plus others depending on bands) is corrected using ancillary fields and absorption cross sections. These steps are typically stable compared to aerosol correction but can still matter in UV/blue bands for hyperspectral sensors.

Graduate point: for hyperspectral retrievals (PACE OCI), small spectral mis-registrations and stray light can create wavelength-dependent artifacts that resemble absorption features; these can leak into both atmospheric and in-water inversions if unmodeled.

(B) Aerosol correction (often the dominant uncertainty)

Heritage open-ocean atmospheric correction (SeaWiFS/MODIS/VIIRS tradition) uses the NIR “black pixel” assumption to estimate aerosol contribution in NIR and extrapolate it into the visible. The classical papers by Gordon & Wang formalize this framework and remain foundational.

\[ \text{Assume } \rho_w(\lambda_{NIR}) \approx 0 \Rightarrow \rho_a(\lambda_{NIR}) \approx \rho_t(\lambda_{NIR}) - \rho_r(\lambda_{NIR}) - \rho_{ra}(\lambda_{NIR}) - \rho_g(\lambda_{NIR}) - \rho_{wc}(\lambda_{NIR}) \]

Then use an aerosol model (or LUT) to transport aerosol reflectance from NIR to VIS via an aerosol “spectral shape” parameter, often expressed using an ε-ratio :

\[ \varepsilon \;=\; \frac{\rho_a(\lambda_1)}{\rho_a(\lambda_2)} \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \rho_a(\lambda_{VIS}) \;=\; \rho_a(\lambda_{NIR}) \cdot f(\lambda_{VIS}, \varepsilon) \]
  • Aerosol model selection: choose a LUT entry (size distribution, refractive index, humidity) consistent with ε and geometry.
  • Multiple scattering: account for Rayleigh–aerosol coupling (ρra) and path transmittance.
  • Iteration: because aerosol selection and water signal are coupled, many implementations iterate to convergence.
Graduate diagnostic: inspect negative Rrs patterns (spectrally and spatially). Systematic negatives in blue often indicate absorbing aerosol / thin cloud contamination or LUT mismatch; negatives clustered near coasts often indicate adjacency and/or bright-water breakdown of the black-pixel assumption.

(C) Surface corrections: glint and whitecaps

Sun glint is modeled from Fresnel reflection and a sea-surface slope distribution (often attributed to Cox–Munk-type parameterizations), driven primarily by wind speed and geometry. Whitecap reflectance is typically parameterized as a wind-speed function with a spectral shape. These are “ancillary-driven” corrections: errors in wind or geometry can map directly into residual artifacts.

  • Glint strategy: avoid (mask), correct (model and subtract), or hybrid (correct where small; mask where large).
  • Whitecaps: most relevant at high winds; can bias green/red reflectance and inflate chlorophyll in some algorithms.

(D) Coastal/turbid and bright-water extensions (where heritage assumptions break)

In coastal/turbid waters, ρw(NIR) ≠ 0 so the black-pixel method overestimates aerosol reflectance and can severely bias visible Rrs. Two common approaches:

  1. NIR iterative correction: jointly estimate aerosol + water reflectance by iterating with a water model.
    Graduate note: this becomes a coupled inverse problem with stronger nonlinearity and parameter covariance (aerosol slope ↔ particle backscattering).
  2. SWIR-based correction: use SWIR bands where water absorption is much stronger so ρw(SWIR) is small again, enabling aerosol estimation even in turbid water.

Additional coastal complications include adjacency effects , subpixel cloud contamination, and rapidly varying aerosols.

(E) Hyperspectral and polarimetric constraints (PACE-relevant perspective)

Hyperspectral sensors provide many contiguous channels, enabling spectral matching and improved detection of subtle artifacts (e.g., stray light, thin cloud). Polarimetric measurements (from co-flying or onboard polarimeters) can constrain aerosol microphysics and surface reflection more tightly than intensity-only measurements, potentially reducing the dominant uncertainty source in standard atmospheric correction.

Graduate viewpoint: in the ideal case, you retrieve aerosol state parameters (AOT, size distribution, refractive index, etc.) using polarimetry + radiative transfer, then condition ocean-color atmospheric correction on those aerosol posteriors—rather than selecting from a discrete aerosol LUT using ε alone.

4.1.2 Uncertainty-aware correction (what “graduate” means here)

A robust graduate-level formulation treats atmospheric correction as an uncertainty-weighted estimation problem. For example, estimate aerosol parameters x by minimizing:

\[ J(x) \;=\; \sum_{\lambda \in \Lambda} \frac{\left[\rho_t^{obs}(\lambda) - \rho_t^{model}(\lambda; x)\right]^2}{\sigma^2_{\rho}(\lambda)} \;+\; \lambda_r\,\|L(x-x_0)\|^2 \]
  • σρ(λ): wavelength-dependent measurement/model uncertainty
  • x0: prior (e.g., climatology, polarimeter-derived aerosol prior)
  • λr, L: regularization strength and operator (e.g., smoothness / physical constraints)
Takeaway: “Better algorithms” are often those that (i) use a more accurate forward model, (ii) correctly weight uncertainties, and (iii) apply physically defensible constraints to keep ill-posed problems stable.

4.2 Empirical chlorophyll algorithms

Empirical chlorophyll algorithms map spectral features of Rrs to chlorophyll concentration (Chl, mg m−3). They are computationally cheap and robust in many open-ocean conditions but can fail where CDOM and sediments vary independently (Case-2 waters).

OCx band-ratio family

A widely used approach relates chlorophyll-a to a blue-to-green reflectance ratio via a log-polynomial model:

\[ \log_{10}(\mathrm{Chl}) = a_0 + a_1 R + a_2 R^{2} + a_3 R^{3} + a_4 R^{4} \]

where \(R\) is often defined as:

\[ R \;=\; \log_{10}\left( \frac{\max\{R_{rs}(\lambda_{blue})\}}{R_{rs}(\lambda_{green})} \right) \]
  • Why it works (Case-1): increasing chlorophyll increases blue absorption → lowers blue reflectance relative to green.
  • Why it fails (Case-2): CDOM absorption and mineral scattering can mimic/challenge the same spectral signature.
  • Graduate practice: always report which coefficient set (sensor + reprocessing) you used; coefficients are not universal.

Color Index (CI) for oligotrophic waters

For very low chlorophyll, blue/green ratios can be noisy and sensitive to atmospheric correction residuals. CI instead measures the deviation of the green band from a baseline between a blue and a red band:

\[ \mathrm{CI} \;=\; R_{rs}(\lambda_g)\;-\;\Big[ R_{rs}(\lambda_b) + \frac{\lambda_g-\lambda_b}{\lambda_r-\lambda_b}\left(R_{rs}(\lambda_r)-R_{rs}(\lambda_b)\right) \Big] \]
  • Interpretation: CI is a curvature measure; it tends to be less sensitive to multiplicative errors in Rrs.
  • Operational note: many processing chains blend CI and OCx across chlorophyll regimes to reduce artifacts.
Naming collision: PACE’s sensor is called OCI . The chlorophyll algorithm “OCI/CI” in the literature refers to an Ocean Color Index concept (not the instrument).

4.2.1 Graduate-level evaluation: validation, robustness, and regimes

  • Regime dependence: evaluate performance separately for oligotrophic gyres, productive open ocean, and coastal/turbid waters.
  • Matchups: use in situ chlorophyll and/or in situ Rrs to separate atmospheric-correction error from in-water algorithm error.
  • Error metrics: use bias, RMSLE/RMSLE*, median absolute percent difference, and stratify by aerosol type and sun glint risk.

4.3 Semi-analytical IOP inversion (Rrs → IOPs → constituents)

Semi-analytical methods explicitly encode a radiative-transfer-inspired relationship between reflectance and IOPs. A common starting point is the relationship between \(u(\lambda)\) and subsurface reflectance:

\[ r_{rs}(\lambda) \approx g_0\,u(\lambda) + g_1\,u(\lambda)^2, \quad u(\lambda)=\frac{b_b(\lambda)}{a(\lambda)+b_b(\lambda)} \]

Then convert between below-surface rrs and above-surface Rrs using an empirical surface transmission relationship (varies by formulation). This gives a forward model to invert for a(λ) and bb(λ), typically after parameterizing spectral shapes.

Graduate caution: spectral similarity causes non-uniqueness. CDOM absorption vs phytoplankton absorption and particle backscattering can trade off. Hyperspectral data help, but priors/constraints (smoothness, non-negativity, spectral libraries) often remain essential.

Algorithm families (what they estimate, and how)

  • GSM family — spectral model fitting (often nonlinear least squares) to retrieve a small parameter set such as Chl, CDOM/detrital absorption amplitude, and particle backscattering amplitude/slope.
    \[ \min_{\theta}\;\sum_{\lambda}\frac{\left[R_{rs}^{obs}(\lambda)-R_{rs}^{model}(\lambda;\theta)\right]^2}{\sigma^2_{Rrs}(\lambda)} \]
  • QAA — stepwise inversion using band relationships and an anchor wavelength to derive a(λ) and bb(λ). QAA is popular because it is fast, reasonably stable, and produces spectral IOPs.
  • GIOP — a flexible inversion framework: choose basis functions (for a_ph, a_dg, bb_p), apply constraints, and fit a forward model to observed Rrs with uncertainty weighting.

4.3.1 Practical inversion details (graduate checklists)

  • Constraints: enforce non-negativity of absorption and backscattering; constrain spectral slopes to physically plausible ranges.
  • Regularization: penalize non-smooth spectra or deviations from priors to stabilize ill-posed inversions.
  • Weighting: weight each wavelength by 1/σ² (instrument SNR + atmospheric correction uncertainty + modeling error).
  • Identifiability tests: examine Jacobians/sensitivities and parameter covariance; do not assume more bands always means more independent information.
  • Closure checks: verify that retrieved IOPs re-predict observed Rrs within uncertainties; large systematic residuals often reveal model mismatch (e.g., unusual particles, shallow bottom, uncorrected glint).
PACE opportunity: hyperspectral OCI can support richer spectral bases (e.g., pigment absorption feature shapes) and better separation of CDOM vs phytoplankton, but only if atmospheric correction preserves spectral fidelity and the inversion uses appropriate priors/constraints.

4.4 Common Level-2 products derived after Rrs

After atmospheric correction yields Rrs(λ), Level-2 products fall broadly into:

  • AOPs: \(K_d(\lambda)\) , PAR-related fields, water clarity indices.
  • BGC proxies: chlorophyll-a, POC/PIC , bloom indices, CDOM-related products.
  • IOPs: retrieved spectral absorption/backscattering components such as a(λ), bb(λ), and their constituent partitions.
Graduate best practice: for any map you present, specify (i) atmospheric correction approach/assumptions (NIR vs SWIR, glint handling), (ii) in-water algorithm family (OCx/CI vs semi-analytical), (iii) uncertainty/flags used, and (iv) expected regime limitations (Case-1 vs Case-2, shallow bottom risk).

5. Major pitfalls and sources of uncertainty

How products fail, and how to diagnose

Ocean-color products are the output of a subtraction-dominated retrieval: we estimate a small oceanic signal by removing much larger atmospheric and surface contributions. As a result, seemingly modest errors (e.g., aerosol model mismatch, glint residuals, small calibration drift) can produce large relative errors in Rrs and amplified errors in derived geophysical products (chlorophyll, IOPs, Kd, etc.).

Graduate mindset: separate (i) measurement error (radiometry, geolocation), (ii) forward-model error (atmospheric/surface/in-water approximations), and (iii) representativeness error (subpixel variability, temporal mismatch). Your “uncertainty” is the combination—and different terms dominate in different regimes (open ocean vs coast, low vs high aerosols).

5.0 A quick diagnostic workflow (first aid for bad-looking products)

  1. Start with context: view an L1B quicklook (or L1-derived RGB) to identify clouds, haze, smoke/dust, and glint geometry.
  2. Interrogate flags: use the Level-2 QA/L2 flags before interpreting maps (do not “eyeball” unfiltered products).
  3. Look at spectra: extract a few Rrs spectra (open ocean, coastal, plume). “Unphysical” shapes are diagnostic: negative blue, sharp band-to-band spikes, or spectrally flat brightening.
  4. Cross-check atmospheric fields: inspect aerosol proxies (AOT/angstrom if available), glint coefficient, cloud/ice masks.
  5. Check spatial patterns: artifacts are often structured: along-track striping (calibration/geometry), nearshore halos (adjacency), sun-glint streaks (geometry/wind), speckle near clouds (thin cloud/cirrus).
Why this works: different failure modes have distinct spectral and spatial signatures. Treat the product like a diagnostic instrument: “What signature does the failure leave behind?”

5.1 Atmospheric and surface contamination

Atmospheric correction errors dominate many ocean-color uncertainties because they affect every in-water product. A helpful way to see error amplification is the simplified relationship:

\[ \rho_w(\lambda) \approx \frac{\rho_t(\lambda)-\rho_{atm}(\lambda)-\rho_{surf}(\lambda)}{T(\lambda)}, \qquad R_{rs}(\lambda) \approx \frac{\rho_w(\lambda)}{\pi} \]

When \(\rho_w\) is small, any bias in \(\rho_{atm}\) or \(\rho_{surf}\) can cause large relative errors in \(R_{rs}\).

5.1.1 Aerosol model mismatch

What it is: the assumed aerosol optical properties (size distribution, refractive index, humidity growth, vertical profile) do not match reality. In heritage processing, this often enters through aerosol LUT selection driven by NIR ratios.

  • Symptom: biased \(R_{rs}(\lambda)\) spectrum, often strongest in the blue; “tilted” spectra (wrong slope) across VIS.
  • Consequence: chlorophyll bias (often large), spurious fronts/gradients, and systematic regional offsets.
  • Spectral signature:
    • Too much aerosol subtraction → negative or near-zero \(R_{rs}\) in blue.
    • Too little subtraction → elevated blue \(R_{rs}\) → unrealistically low chlorophyll in OCx/CI regimes.
  • Spatial signature: broad regional biases aligned with aerosol plumes, dust outbreaks, smoke layers, or synoptic haze.
Graduate diagnostics: (i) compare \(R_{rs}\) spectra against physically plausible shapes for the water type, (ii) check for coherent biases across multiple adjacent bands (aerosol mismatch is usually smooth spectrally), and (iii) examine whether failure coincides with elevated AOT or unusual aerosol events (dust/smoke).

Mitigation strategies: filter high-aerosol scenes; use improved aerosol constraints (e.g., polarimetry-informed aerosols when available); prefer SWIR-assisted correction in bright/turbid waters; and validate by comparing against in situ \(R_{rs}\) where possible.

5.1.2 Absorbing aerosols and thin cirrus

What it is: aerosols with significant absorption (e.g., smoke) and/or subpixel or thin cirrus layers that are not fully detected/masked. These cases break assumptions used by many intensity-only aerosol corrections and can be difficult to flag.

  • Symptom: depressed blue; patchy artifacts; negative \(R_{rs}\) at short wavelengths; “milky” or washed-out scenes.
  • Consequence: inflated chlorophyll (blue reflectance too low) and unstable semi-analytical inversions (unphysical IOP partitions).

  • Spatial signature:
Graduate diagnostics: (i) look for “blue-only” failures (negatives primarily in shortest bands), (ii) check proximity to clouds and cloud edges (thin cirrus and adjacency often co-occur), and (iii) compare multiple nearby pixels: thin cirrus artifacts often vary on small spatial scales.

Mitigation strategies: conservative cloud/cirrus masking; stricter filtering near cloud edges; use additional spectral tests (including NIR/SWIR consistency) where available; and leverage polarimetric constraints for aerosols when possible.

What it is: sun glint adds a bright, often spectrally broad contribution to TOA reflectance that can be only partially corrected.

  • Symptom: bright streaks; elevated reflectance across bands; chlorophyll artifacts; “white” sheen on imagery.

5.2 In-water algorithm limitations

5.2.1 Case-2 waters (independent CDOM/sediments variability)

Graduate exercise: propagate Rrs uncertainty into OCx chlorophyll uncertainty

For OCx, let \(R=\log_{10}(B/G)\) where \(B\) and \(G\) are blue and green \(R_{rs}\). If uncertainties are small and independent, a first-order approximation is:

\[ \sigma_R \approx \frac{1}{\ln 10}\sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_B}{B}\right)^2+\left(\frac{\sigma_G}{G}\right)^2} \]

If \(\log_{10}(\mathrm{Chl}) = a_0+a_1R+a_2R^2+a_3R^3+a_4R^4\), then:

\[ \sigma_{\log_{10}\mathrm{Chl}} \approx \left|a_1+2a_2R+3a_3R^2+4a_4R^3\right|\,\sigma_R \]

This shows why small relative errors in \(R_{rs}\) can translate into large chlorophyll uncertainty, especially in regimes where the polynomial slope is steep.

Continuity, cross-calibration, and climate records

Ocean color has a multi-decade record. Building consistent climate records requires careful merging of sensors with different spectral bands, calibration strategies, and viewing geometries.

7. Introduction to PACE

Hyperspectral ocean color + polarimetry

PACE is designed to advance ocean-color biogeochemistry and atmospheric science jointly, improving our ability to correct atmospheric effects and interpret ocean ecosystem change.

7.1 Instruments

  • OCI — a hyperspectral UV–VIS–NIR instrument with SWIR bands for atmospheric correction/aerosol work.
  • SPEXone — multi-angle polarimetry to improve aerosol/cloud characterization.
  • HARP2 — wide-angle imaging polarimetry for aerosols/clouds and surface properties.

7.2 What changes relative to heritage missions?

  • Hyperspectral OCI enables improved discrimination of phytoplankton spectral features and more flexible inversion constraints.
  • Polarimetry provides stronger aerosol constraints, potentially reducing one of the largest uncertainty sources in ocean color.
  • UV–NIR–SWIR coverage expands correction and retrieval options in challenging waters (coastal, turbid, absorbing aerosols).
Data mindset: Always track processing version and flags. For any mission, early releases can change materially as calibration and algorithms mature.

8.2 Plotting Level-1B (TOA) vs Level-2 (Rrs and chlorophyll)

Goal: visually demonstrate what atmospheric correction accomplished by comparing a TOA reflectance slice ( Level-1B ) with an Rrs slice ( Level-2 ), and then mapping a derived geophysical product (chlor_a).

Python environment setup

conda create -n pace-oc python=3.11 -y
conda activate pace-oc
pip install numpy xarray netCDF4 matplotlib cartopy dask

Helper: open netCDF groups safely

PACE/OBPG files are netCDF4 and often use groups such as geophysical_data and navigation_data. 

import xarray as xr

def open_group(fn, group):
    """Open a netCDF group (OBPG-style netCDF4 with groups)."""
    return xr.open_dataset(fn, group=group, decode_cf=True, mask_and_scale=True)

Plot Level-1B TOA reflectance near 443 nm

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

l1b_file = "PACE_OCI.20240415T150000.L1B.nc"  # replace with your file

geo   = open_group(l1b_file, "geolocation_data")
obs   = open_group(l1b_file, "observation_data")
bands = open_group(l1b_file, "sensor_band_parameters")

lat = geo["latitude"]
lon = geo["longitude"]

wl_blue = bands["blue_wavelength"].values
i443 = int(np.argmin(np.abs(wl_blue - 443)))

# rhot_* are TOA reflectances
rhot_443 = obs["rhot_blue"].isel(blue_bands=i443)

plt.figure()
plt.pcolormesh(lon, lat, rhot_443, shading="auto")
plt.title("PACE OCI L1B: TOA reflectance (rhot) near 443 nm")
plt.xlabel("Longitude")
plt.ylabel("Latitude")
plt.colorbar(label="unitless (TOA reflectance)")
plt.show()
Interpretation: Bright features in L1B can be clouds, haze, glint, or aerosols—not chlorophyll. You are still looking at TOA.
