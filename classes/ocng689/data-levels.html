Data Levels in Satellite Oceanography

From raw to analysis-ready

Satellite measurements are processed through standardized levels to support consistent data stewardship and user interpretation. The central question each level answers is: how far is this dataset from the original measurement?

Common satellite data processing levels (oceanography examples)
Level Name Typical contents Common use
0 Raw data Reconstructed instrument packets/counts with minimal processing. Archival baseline; reprocessing; engineering validation.
1 Calibrated & geolocated Radiances/brightness temperatures/backscatter; navigation applied. Algorithm development; quality assessment; retrieval inputs.
2 Geophysical products SST, chlorophyll, wind vectors, SSH along tracks/swaths; QC flags. Process studies; validation; high-fidelity local analyses.
3 Gridded composites Mapped fields on a standard grid (daily/weekly/monthly). Climatologies; mapping; broad-scale comparisons.
4 Analyzed / model-integrated Gap-free analyses (often multi-sensor) and/or assimilated products. Model forcing/validation; operational products; consistent fields.

2.5.1 Level 0: Raw Data

Level 0 is reconstructed, unprocessed instrument data at full resolution. It is not “science-ready” and often requires mission-specific metadata and tools, but it is essential for preserving the closest representation of what the instrument measured.

Examples:
  • MODIS Level 0: Raw digital counts from all 36 spectral bands before any calibration
  • Jason-3 Level 0: Altimeter waveform samples and telemetry packets
  • Sentinel-1 Level 0: Raw SAR echo data (I/Q samples) before focusing

2.5.2 Level 1: Calibrated and Geolocated Data

Level 1 data are radiometrically calibrated and (typically) geolocated. Examples include calibrated radiances, brightness temperatures, radar backscatter, or altimeter waveforms/ranges with navigation information.

Examples:
  • MODIS L1B: Top-of-atmosphere radiances (W m⁻² sr⁻¹ μm⁻¹) with geolocation for each pixel
  • AMSR2 L1R: Brightness temperatures (K) at multiple microwave frequencies
  • Sentinel-3 OLCI L1: Calibrated radiances in 21 spectral bands with per-pixel lat/lon
  • Jason-3 L1: Retracked altimeter range, sigma0, and significant wave height per along-track point

2.5.3 Level 2: Geophysical Products

Level 2 products provide derived geophysical variables at the measurement resolution and sampling geometry (e.g., along-track or swath). Atmospheric corrections, auxiliary meteorology, geophysical models (e.g., tides), and quality flags are often incorporated at this stage.

Examples:
  • MODIS L2 SST: Sea surface temperature (°C) at 1 km resolution, swath geometry, with quality flags
  • MODIS L2 OC: Chlorophyll-a concentration (mg m⁻³), remote sensing reflectance Rrs(λ) at native resolution
  • ASCAT L2: Ocean surface wind vectors (speed and direction) along the scatterometer swath
  • Jason-3 L2: Sea surface height anomaly (SSHA), geostrophic currents along the ground track
  • SMAP L2: Sea surface salinity (PSU) at ~40 km resolution in swath coordinates

2.5.4 Higher Levels: Gridded and Analyzed Data

Level 3 maps Level 2 variables onto consistent grids, often as space–time composites. Level 4 adds analysis methods and/or model constraints (including data assimilation) to produce gap-free fields. These levels are convenient for mapping and modeling but can be farther from the instrument measurement and may incorporate smoothing or model structure.

Level 3 Examples:
  • MODIS L3 SST: Daily/8-day/monthly SST composites on a 4 km global grid
  • MODIS L3 CHL: Chlorophyll-a mapped to 4 km or 9 km grids (daily, 8-day, monthly)
  • CCMP Winds: 6-hourly gridded ocean winds from multiple satellites on 0.25° grid
  • AVISO SSHA: Along-track altimetry mapped to regular grids (weekly or monthly)
Level 4 Examples:
  • GHRSST MUR SST: Daily gap-free 1 km SST analysis blending multiple IR and MW sensors
  • AVISO DUACS: Multi-mission altimeter gridded sea level anomaly (merged Jason, Sentinel-6, etc.)
  • GLORYS: Global ocean reanalysis with satellite altimetry, SST, and sea ice assimilated into a model
  • ECCO: MIT GCM-based state estimate constrained by satellite and in situ observations

Interpretation caution: Higher levels are easier to use, but they may blend sensors, fill gaps, and impose assumptions. If you need “closest-to-observation” constraints (e.g., algorithm validation), start from Level 1/2 when feasible.

2.6 Major Data Distribution Centers for Satellite Oceanography

Where you actually get the data

Professional practice in satellite oceanography includes knowing where authoritative datasets are archived and how to evaluate product maturity, processing level, latency, and documentation quality.

2.6.1 NASA Earthdata

NASA Earthdata provides discovery and access to NASA Earth observation holdings, supported by discipline-specific Distributed Active Archive Centers (DAACs). Oceanography-relevant DAACs include PO.DAAC (physical oceanography) and OB.DAAC (ocean biology/ocean color). Common workflows include portal download, APIs, and increasingly cloud-optimized analysis.

2.6.2 NOAA CoastWatch

NOAA CoastWatch provides near-real-time and operational ocean products, including SST and ocean color, often organized by latency and product family. This is particularly useful for applications requiring rapid delivery (e.g., hazards, operational monitoring).

2.6.3 ESA Copernicus Hub

Copernicus Sentinel mission data are distributed through the Copernicus Data Space Ecosystem. For many users, the practical workflow is: search → select product level and timeliness → download or access via API → process in Python using netCDF/GeoTIFF/SAFE formats.

2.6.4 Other Centers

Additional important centers for ocean remote sensing include JAXA G-Portal and CNES AVISO+ (altimetry services), among others.

Practical note (Python): Most satellite ocean products are distributed as netCDF/HDF (or mission-specific packaging). Core skills include reading metadata, applying quality flags, handling projections/geolocation, and tracking processing level and version.
